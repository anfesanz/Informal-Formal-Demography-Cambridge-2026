A
Abridged life table
Day 2Day 3Mortality

A life table that uses grouped age intervals (typically 0, 1–4, 5–9, 10–14, … in five-year bands) rather than single years of age. The columns are identical to a complete life table — \({}_nM_x\), \({}_nq_x\), \({}_na_x\), \(l_x\), \({}_nd_x\), \({}_nL_x\), \(T_x\), \(e_x\) — but each row covers \(n\) years rather than 1.

The interval width \(n\) matters: using \(n=5\) loses information about mortality within each band. The \({}_na_x\) parameter ("average years lived in the interval by those who die in it") partially corrects for this. Most published life tables (including those from the HMD) are in abridged form for older age groups.

Age-at-death distribution \(f(x)\)
Day 2Mortality

The density function describing the distribution of deaths by age. It is the negative derivative of the survivorship function, equal to the product of the force of mortality and the survival function:

\[ f(x) = -l'(x) = \mu(x)\cdot l(x) \]

Since \(l(x)\) decreases from 1 to 0, \(f(x)\) integrates to 1 and is a proper probability density. Historical shape: in pre-industrial populations, \(f(x)\) was bimodal — a large infant spike (many deaths in the first year) and a smaller hump at older ages. In modern populations it is roughly bell-shaped, concentrated in late old age. The progressive compression of deaths into old age is called the "rectangularisation" of the survival curve.

→ Day 2, §5
Age-specific fertility rate (ASFR) \({}_nF_x\)
Day 3Fertility

The number of births per woman-year of exposure in age group \([x, x+n)\):

\[ {}_nF_x = \frac{\text{births to women aged } [x,x+n)}{{}_nK_x^{\text{female}}} \]

Age-specific rates cover the reproductive ages 15–49 (sometimes 10–54). The ASFR schedule describes the shape of childbearing — high-fertility societies start earlier and continue later; low-fertility societies show a narrow peak around age 28–32.

Common mistake: when summing discrete ASFRs to get the TFR, you must multiply each rate by the interval width \(n\) — the TFR is the area under the fertility curve, not the sum of the heights.

Real-world: England and Wales (2020) peak ASFR ≈ 95 per 1,000 women at ages 30–34. Niger peak ≈ 300+ per 1,000 in the early 20s.

→ Day 3, §6
Age-specific mortality rate \({}_nM_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The number of deaths in age group \([x, x+n)\) per person-year of exposure in that group:

\[ {}_nM_x = \frac{{}_nd_x}{{}_nL_x} \]

This is the rate that vital registration systems produce — the observable quantity from real data. The denominator is person-years \({}_nL_x\), not the number alive at the start of the interval \(l_x\). This distinction is crucial: if half the deaths happen early in the interval, those people contribute less than \(n\) person-years each.

Rate ≠ probability: \({}_nM_x\) is a rate; \({}_nq_x\) is a probability. Converting between them requires an assumption about how deaths are distributed within the interval (typically UDD: uniform distribution of deaths, giving \({}_na_x = n/2\)).

→ Day 2, §7 · Day 2, §9
Average person-years lived by deaths \({}_na_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The average number of years lived within interval \([x, x+n)\) by individuals who die in that interval. It fills the gap between the endpoints: if someone dies at age \(x+a\) where \(0 \leq a \leq n\), their contribution to person-years is \(a\), not \(n\). The average over all deaths in the interval is \({}_na_x\).

\[ {}_na_x = \frac{{}_nL_x - n\cdot l_{x+n}}{{}_nd_x} \]

UDD assumption: if deaths are uniformly distributed within the interval, \({}_na_x = n/2\). This fails at age 0 (infant deaths cluster in the first weeks of life, so \({}_1a_0 \approx 0.1\text{–}0.3\)) and at the very oldest ages. The value of \({}_na_x\) is critical for converting observed rates \({}_nM_x\) to probabilities \({}_nq_x\): \({}_nq_x = n\,{}_nM_x / (1 + (n - {}_na_x)\,{}_nM_x)\).

Age pyramid (population pyramid)
Day 4Day 5Method

A back-to-back horizontal bar chart showing the number (or proportion) of males and females in each age group — males on the left, females on the right, age increasing upward. Three archetypal shapes emerge from the stable population formula: a wide-based pyramid (\(r > 0\), high growth); a near-rectangular column (\(r \approx 0\), stationary); and an urn or inverted shape (\(r < 0\), sustained sub-replacement decline).

In the stable population, the age distribution is fully determined by \(r\) and the survival function:

\[ c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x) \]

A higher \(r\) steepens the \(e^{-rx}\) discount and compresses the pyramid base inward; a negative \(r\) reverses the gradient. The age pyramid is therefore the visual signature of demographic history — a youth bulge marks past high fertility; a large elderly cohort marks past fertility decline or post-war mortality improvement.

Demographic dividend: the transitional phase when the working-age population is large relative to both children and elderly (both dependency groups small). If well-managed through investment in education and institutions, this "demographic window" can fuel rapid economic growth — as seen in East Asia from the 1960s–90s.

B
Baby Boom
Day 3Growth

The surge in birth rates in the United States (and many other high-income countries) following World War II, conventionally defined as 1946–1964. The boom began in 1946 as soldiers returned and families resumed plans deferred during the war; it ended around 1964 as the contraceptive pill became widely available and the cultural moment shifted.

The leading edge of the Baby Boomers reached retirement age from about 2011, creating the "silver tsunami" now straining pension systems, healthcare infrastructure, and labour markets across the developed world. The cohort illustrates why age structure matters: even when NRR is near 1, a bulge cohort can drive decades of social and economic change as it ages through the system.

Lexis diagram note: the Baby Boom appears as a thickened diagonal band in any Lexis representation of US births — or as a pronounced spike in a plot of births by year.

→ Day 3, §1
Balancing equation
Day 1Growth

The fundamental accounting identity of demography. For any population over any time interval:

\[ P_{\text{now}} = P_{\text{before}} + \text{Births} - \text{Deaths} + \text{In-migrants} - \text{Out-migrants} \]

There are exactly four "doors" through which a population can change — births, deaths, in-migration, and out-migration — and no others. The equation is always true by definition; it underlies every demographic model, projection, and estimate.

For a closed population (no migration), it simplifies to \(P_{\text{now}} = P_{\text{before}} + B - D\). In practice, adjustment and residual error terms are added to reconcile different data sources (census vs. vital registration).

→ Day 1, §1
Brass relational logit model
Day 3MortalityMethod

A two-parameter model for representing any survival schedule as a transformation of a standard schedule \(S_0(x)\), introduced by Sir William Brass (1921–2001):

\[ \text{logit}\,S(x) = \alpha + \beta\,\text{logit}\,S_0(x) \]

where \(\text{logit}\,p = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{p}{1-p}\). The parameter \(\alpha\) shifts the overall level of mortality (positive \(\alpha\) → higher mortality); \(\beta\) changes the age pattern (values above 1 concentrate mortality at younger or older ages relative to the standard).

Brass was motivated by the reality that many developing countries lacked complete death registration, but partial data — child mortality surveys, census questions about survival of children — could still identify the two parameters. His methods remain foundational in Sub-Saharan African demography and indirect estimation more broadly.

→ Day 3, §2
C
Carrying capacity \(\kappa\)
Day 1Growth

The maximum population size that an environment (or resource base) can sustain indefinitely. In the logistic growth model, \(\kappa\) is the ceiling that the population asymptotically approaches:

\[ \frac{dK}{dt} = rK\!\left(1 - \frac{K}{\kappa}\right) \]

Population growth is fastest at \(K = \kappa/2\) (the inflection point of the S-curve), then decelerates as resources become scarce. In ecology, \(\kappa\) depends on food availability, space, and predation. In human demography, the concept has had mixed reception — technological progress (the Green Revolution, synthetic fertilisers) has repeatedly raised the practical ceiling, making it hard to estimate empirically.

→ Day 1, §13
Censoring
Day 3Method

In survival analysis, a person is censored when their event time is not fully observed. Right censoring (most common): the person is still alive when observation ends — you know they survived at least \(t\) years but not how long after. Left censoring: the event occurred before the study began — you know it happened, but not when.

Ignoring censored individuals biases survival estimates downward by under-counting person-time. The Kaplan-Meier estimator handles right censoring correctly by including partial contributions to person-time: each person contributes exposure up to their last known alive time, then exits the risk set.

→ Day 3, §4
Cohort
Day 2Method

A group of individuals who share a common defining event within a specific time period — most commonly, a birth cohort (all persons born in a given year or range of years). On the Lexis diagram, a birth cohort occupies a diagonal band slanting upward to the right.

Cohort vs. period: cohort analysis follows the same people through time (diagonal in the Lexis diagram); period analysis takes a snapshot across all ages at one moment (vertical slice). Cohort life expectancy requires waiting decades for a cohort to die out; period life expectancy uses current rates applied to a synthetic cohort — the two differ when mortality is changing.

→ Day 2, §3
Cox proportional hazards model
Day 3Method

A semi-parametric regression model for survival data, introduced by Sir David Cox (1972). The hazard for individual \(i\) with covariates \(\mathbf{Z}\) is:

\[ \mu(x \mid \mathbf{Z}) = \mu_0(x)\cdot\exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}\cdot\mathbf{Z}) \]

The baseline hazard \(\mu_0(x)\) can take any shape (the "semi-parametric" part); the covariate effect is multiplicative. "Proportional hazards" means the hazard ratio between any two individuals is constant over time: \(\mu_i(x)/\mu_j(x) = \exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}\cdot(\mathbf{Z}_i - \mathbf{Z}_j))\) regardless of age \(x\).

The most widely used survival regression model in medicine and epidemiology. In demography, it appears in studies linking individual-level characteristics (education, income, smoking) to mortality risk. The product formula \(l(x) = \prod {}_np\) is the demographic analogue of the Cox baseline survival function.

Crude birth / death rate b, d
Day 1Growth

The number of births (or deaths) per person per unit time, ignoring age structure:

\[ b = \frac{\text{Births}}{K(t)}, \qquad d = \frac{\text{Deaths}}{K(t)} \]

Called "crude" because every person is in the denominator equally — a newborn counts the same as a 40-year-old. This makes crude rates misleading for cross-country comparisons: a country with an older age structure will have a higher crude death rate even if its age-specific mortality is lower.

UK example (2020): crude birth rate ≈ 10.5/1,000; crude death rate ≈ 9.4/1,000 → crude rate of natural increase ≈ 1.1/1,000 (0.11% per year).

→ Day 1, §3
Cumulative hazard \(H(x)\)
Day 3Mortality

The total accumulated mortality hazard from birth to age \(x\) — the area under the \(\mu(x)\) curve:

\[ H(x) = \int_0^x \mu(y)\,dy \]

The survival function is then \(l(x) = e^{-H(x)}\). Because \(\mu(y) \geq 0\), \(H(x)\) is non-decreasing and \(l(x)\) is non-increasing. Think of \(H(x)\) as the "total lifetime dose" of mortality risk received by surviving to age \(x\). It is also written \(\Lambda(x)\) in the biostatistics literature.

Gompertz example: with \(\mu(x) = ae^{bx}\), the cumulative hazard is \(H(x) = (a/b)(e^{bx} - 1)\) — it rises exponentially with age.

→ Day 3, §1
D
Deaths in age interval \({}_nd_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The proportion of the original cohort dying between exact ages \(x\) and \(x+n\):

\[ {}_nd_x = l_x - l_{x+n} \]

Alternatively, \({}_nd_x = l_x \cdot {}_nq_x\). Because \(l_x\) and \(l_{x+n}\) are fractions of the original cohort (radix = 1), \({}_nd_x\) is also a fraction and all \({}_nd_x\) values sum to 1.

Plotted as a histogram against age, the \({}_nd_x\) column gives the age-at-death distribution — a vivid summary of where in the life course deaths occur. In pre-industrial populations, the histogram shows a large spike at age 0–1 and a hump at old age; modern populations show almost all deaths concentrated in old age.

→ Day 2, §6
Death probability \({}_nq_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The probability that a person alive at exact age \(x\) dies before reaching age \(x+n\):

\[ {}_nq_x = \frac{{}_nd_x}{l_x} = 1 - \frac{l_{x+n}}{l_x} = 1 - {}_np_x \]

This is a conditional probability (conditioned on being alive at \(x\)). It differs from the mortality rate \({}_nM_x\) in an important way: the denominator of \({}_nq_x\) is \(l_x\) (everyone alive at the start), while the denominator of \({}_nM_x\) is \({}_nL_x\) (total person-years lived during the interval). Converting \({}_nM_x\) to \({}_nq_x\) requires the UDD assumption or knowledge of \({}_na_x\).

Demographic transition
Day 1Growth

The historical shift experienced by all countries as they develop, from a high-mortality, high-fertility equilibrium to a low-mortality, low-fertility equilibrium:

Stage 1: High birth rates and high (and variable) death rates → slow, fluctuating population growth. Stage 2: Death rates fall (public health, nutrition, medicine) while birth rates remain high → rapid population growth. Stage 3: Birth rates fall (education, contraception, urbanisation) → growth decelerates. Stage 4: Both rates low → near-stationary population.

The 20th-century global population explosion (from ~1.6 billion in 1900 to ~8 billion in 2023) is explained almost entirely by the Stage 2 gap — mortality fell faster than fertility did. The theory was formalised by Frank Notestein (1945).

→ Day 1, §14
Doubling time \(T_2 = \ln 2 / r\)
Day 1Growth

The time for a population growing at continuous rate \(r\) to double. From \(K_0 e^{rT_2} = 2K_0\):

\[ T_2 = \frac{\ln 2}{r} \approx \frac{0.693}{r} \approx \frac{70}{r\%} \]

The Rule of 70: divide 70 by the percentage growth rate to get the approximate doubling time in years. At \(r = 1\%\): \(T_2 \approx 70\) years. At \(r = 2\%\): \(T_2 \approx 35\) years. At global peak growth (~2% in the late 1960s), world population doubled in roughly a generation. Use 72 for precision at higher rates (> 6%).

E
Epidemiological transition
Day 2Mortality

The historical shift in the dominant causes of death, from infectious and parasitic diseases (cholera, smallpox, tuberculosis) to chronic non-communicable diseases (cardiovascular disease, cancer) at older ages. Described by Abdel Omran (1971).

The transition is linked to the demographic transition: falling infant and child mortality from infectious diseases is Stage 2. As the population ages, chronic diseases become the leading killers. The epidemiological transition also explains the "rectangularisation" of the \(l(x)\) survival curve — deaths become increasingly compressed into a narrow window at old age.

Some scholars identify a "fourth stage" where cardiovascular disease mortality itself declines (due to better treatment), pushing \(e_0\) above 80 in high-income countries.

→ Day 2, §5 (callout)
Life expectancy at age x \(e_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The expected number of additional years of life for a person who has already reached exact age \(x\):

\[ e_x = \frac{T_x}{l_x} = \frac{\int_x^\omega l(a)\,da}{l(x)} \]

Warning: when agencies report "life expectancy at birth," they almost always mean period life expectancy — a synthetic measure using current mortality rates, not a forecast. During periods of improving mortality, period \(e_0\) underestimates the lifespan a newborn will actually experience.

Real-world (2020): UK period \(e_0\): female ≈ 83, male ≈ 79. Japan female ≈ 88. Sierra Leone ≈ 55.

Stationary population identity: \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\) — the birth rate times life expectancy equals 1 (Little's Law).

→ Day 2, §8
Eigenvalue / eigenvector \(\lambda_1, \mathbf{v}\)
Day 4Day 5Method

For a matrix \(\mathbf{A}\), a scalar \(\lambda\) and a vector \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\) satisfy \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\) — the matrix scales the vector without rotating it. In the Leslie matrix, the dominant eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) (the largest in absolute value) determines the long-run growth rate: \(r = \ln\lambda_1\). The corresponding right eigenvector gives the stable age distribution \(c(x)\); the left eigenvector gives reproductive value \(v(x)\).

Sub-dominant eigenvalues \(\lambda_2, \lambda_3, \ldots\) control the transient oscillations as the population converges to its stable structure — "echoes" of the initial age distribution that decay at rate \(|\lambda_k/\lambda_1|^t \to 0\). The Perron-Frobenius theorem guarantees that \(\lambda_1\) is real, positive, and unique for any realistic Leslie matrix.

Rule of thumb: \(\lambda_1 \approx \text{NRR}^{1/\bar{\mu}}\), so \(r = \ln\lambda_1 \approx \ln(\text{NRR})/\bar{\mu}\). This connects the algebraic quantity (eigenvalue) to the demographic intuition (generation-time compounding of the NRR).

Euler-Lotka equation
Day 4Day 5Method

The characteristic equation that defines the intrinsic growth rate \(r\) of a stable population. In the stable state, births grow as \(B(t) = B(0)e^{rt}\). Substituting this into the renewal equation (total births = women in each age group × their fertility) and simplifying yields:

\[ 1 = \int_{15}^{50} e^{-rx}\,\phi(x)\,dx = \int_{15}^{50} e^{-rx}\,f(x)\,\frac{l(x)}{l(0)}\,F_{\text{FAB}}\,dx \]

This is one equation in one unknown (\(r\)) and must generally be solved numerically. Setting \(r = 0\) recovers NRR = 1 — exact replacement is the \(r = 0\) case. The rule-of-thumb approximation \(r \approx \ln(\text{NRR})/\bar{\mu}\) comes from expanding \(e^{-rx}\) to first order around the mean age of childbearing \(\bar{\mu}\).

History: Leonhard Euler sketched the idea in a 1760 letter to a German clergyman (Johann Peter Süssmilch); Alfred Lotka placed it on rigorous footing in 1907. The equation is also called the "renewal equation" — births are the "renewal" of the population. Structurally identical equations appear in queueing theory, reliability engineering, and epidemiology (\(R_0 = 1\) for disease invasion).

→ Day 4, §5–§6 · Day 5, §2
Exponential growth \(K(t) = K_0 e^{rt}\)
Day 1Growth

The continuous-time limit of geometric growth, arising when population changes at a constant instantaneous rate \(r\):

\[ K(t) = K_0\,e^{rt}, \qquad \frac{dK}{dt} = r\,K(t) \]

On a log-population plot, exponential growth is a straight line with slope \(r\). In practice, populations cannot grow exponentially indefinitely — they are bounded by resources (logistic model) or by the demographic transition. Thomas Malthus (1798) first argued that human populations tend to grow exponentially while food production grows arithmetically, leading to periodic famine-checks on growth.

F
Fertility transition
Day 3Fertility

The historical decline in average births per woman from pre-industrial levels (TFR ~5–7) to near-replacement (~2.1) or below. Typically follows the mortality decline of the demographic transition by one or two generations — parents adjust their fertility downward once child survival improves and the economic returns to large families decline.

In many high-income countries the transition is complete and TFR has fallen well below replacement level (South Korea: 0.84 in 2023; Japan: ~1.2). In Sub-Saharan Africa it is still ongoing, with TFRs of 5–7 in some countries. The timing and pace of the fertility transition is the primary determinant of future population size.

→ Day 3, §6
Force of mortality (hazard rate) \(\mu(x)\)
Day 2Day 3Mortality

The instantaneous rate of dying at exact age \(x\), given survival to that age — the limit of the age-specific mortality rate as interval width shrinks to zero:

\[ \mu(x) = \lim_{n\to 0} {}_nM_x = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln l(x) = \frac{-l'(x)}{l(x)} \]

The survival function and force of mortality are in one-to-one correspondence: \(l(x) = e^{-\int_0^x \mu(y)\,dy}\). This relationship — the survival ODE \(dl/dx = -\mu(x)\,l(x)\) solved by separation of variables — is the mathematical heart of mortality analysis.

The Gompertz model (\(\mu(x) = ae^{bx}\)) fits many human populations well; hazard roughly doubles every 8–10 years in adults. The Makeham extension adds a constant background hazard for accidents.

→ Day 2, §11 · Day 3, §1
Fourier series
Day 2Method

A representation of any periodic function as a sum of sines and cosines:

\[ f(x) \approx a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^N \left[a_n\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi nx}{T}\right) + b_n\sin\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi nx}{T}\right)\right] \]

In demography, Fourier analysis is used to detect seasonal cycles in births and deaths. More broadly, Fourier and Taylor series are complementary: Taylor approximates smooth functions locally with polynomials; Fourier approximates periodic functions globally with sinusoids. They are connected via Euler's formula: \(e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x\), which shows the exponential and trigonometric functions are aspects of the same mathematical object.

Introduced by Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier (1768–1830). His 1807 memoir on heat conduction was initially rejected by Lagrange and Laplace. Fourier analysis is now the foundation of signal processing, MRI, and data compression (JPEG).

→ Day 2, §1
Fraction female at birth \(F_{\text{FAB}} \approx 0.4878\)
Day 3Fertility

The proportion of all live births that are female. The sex ratio at birth is approximately 105 males per 100 females in most populations, giving:

\[ F_{\text{FAB}} = \frac{100}{100 + 105} \approx 0.4878 \]

Demographic reproduction is conventionally tracked through the female line — only daughters can themselves become mothers. Therefore all reproduction measures that focus on daughters (GRR, NRR, net maternity function) incorporate \(F_{\text{FAB}}\) as a multiplicative factor to convert from total births (TFR) to daughter births (GRR = \(F_{\text{FAB}} \times \text{TFR}\)).

Common mistake: forgetting \(F_{\text{FAB}}\) when computing the NRR gives a result roughly twice the correct value.

→ Day 3, §9
Frailty (unobserved)
Day 3Mortality

Hidden variation in mortality risk between individuals. The standard life table assumes all individuals in an age group face the same hazard — an approximation that ignores the biological reality that some people are constitutionally more robust and some more frail. In a multiplicative frailty model (Vaupél, Manton & Stallard, 1979), individual \(i\)'s hazard is:

\[ \mu_i(x) = z_i \cdot \mu_0(x), \qquad z_i \sim \text{Gamma}(1, \sigma^2) \]

Frail individuals (high \(z\)) die earlier; robust individuals (low \(z\)) survive longer. As frail people die preferentially, the surviving population becomes increasingly robust — so the observed population-average hazard declines with age faster than any individual's true hazard. This means life table mortality rates in old age understate the individual hazard that survivors actually face. Frailty is a major theme in longevity research and a reason why Gompertz models eventually "decelerate" at very old ages in empirical data.

→ Day 3, §3 (callout)
G
Geometric growth \(K_t = K_0(1+r)^t\)
Day 1Growth

Discrete-time population growth where the population is multiplied by a fixed factor each period:

\[ K_t = K_0(1+r)^t \]

The average growth rate over a period is the geometric mean, not the arithmetic mean: \(\bar{r} = (K_t/K_0)^{1/t} - 1\). This matters because arithmetic averaging overstates growth when rates vary. Example: +50% then −50% gives a geometric mean of −13%, not zero. For small \(r\) (< 5%), geometric and exponential growth are nearly indistinguishable: \((1+r)^t \approx e^{rt}\) by Taylor expansion.

→ Day 1, §4
Gompertz model \(\mu(x) = ae^{bx}\)
Day 2Mortality

The most widely used parametric model for adult human mortality, introduced by Benjamin Gompertz (1825). The hazard rises exponentially with age:

\[ \mu(x) = a\,e^{bx} \]

\(a\) sets the baseline level; \(b\) sets the rate of ageing. In human populations, \(b \approx 0.08\text{–}0.10\), implying the hazard roughly doubles every 7–9 years. The Makeham extension adds a constant background: \(\mu(x) = A + Be^{cx}\), capturing mortality from accidents which does not increase with age.

Empirically, the Gompertz model fits well from about age 30 to 80; at very old ages (90+) the hazard decelerates, partly due to frailty selection (the most frail have already died).

→ Day 2, §11
Gross Reproduction Ratio (GRR) GRR
Day 3Fertility

The average number of daughters a woman would have over her lifetime under current age-specific fertility rates, assuming no woman dies before the end of the reproductive period:

\[ \text{GRR} = F_{\text{FAB}} \int_{15}^{50} f(x)\,dx = F_{\text{FAB}} \cdot \text{TFR} \]

GRR counts only daughters because only daughters can themselves reproduce. It ignores mortality — it is a "mortality-free" upper bound on reproduction. The NRR corrects this by weighting the fertility schedule by survivorship. Always: NRR ≤ GRR; the ratio NRR/GRR ≈ \(l(\bar{\mu})/l(0)\), the fraction of women surviving through their reproductive years.

→ Day 3, §7–§8
H
Human Mortality Database (HMD)
Day 3Method

The primary open-access repository of historical mortality and population data, available at mortality.org. Maintained jointly by UC Berkeley and the Max Planck Institute for Demographic Research (founded 2000). Covers ~40 countries, some series going back to the 17th century. Provides harmonised life table data, death rates, and population exposures in standardised format, enabling rigorous cross-country and historical comparisons.

The HMD is the standard reference for academic mortality research and is cited in virtually every study of longevity trends. Data from England and Wales back to 1841 allow comparisons between pre-industrial and modern mortality regimes — a core exercise in the Day 3 notes.

→ Day 3, §5
I
Infant mortality rate (IMR) \({}_1q_0\)
Day 2Mortality

The probability of dying before age 1, expressed per 1,000 live births:

\[ \text{IMR} = {}_1q_0 = \frac{\text{deaths under age 1}}{\text{live births}} \]

The most sensitive part of the life table — infant mortality is the first measure to improve as countries develop, and one of the last to stop falling. Pre-industrial rates: 200–400 per 1,000. Modern high-income countries: under 5 per 1,000. Afghanistan, Sierra Leone: still 50–60 per 1,000.

The first year of life requires special treatment in the life table because deaths cluster heavily in the first weeks (\({}_1a_0 \approx 0.1\text{–}0.3\), well below the UDD assumption of 0.5). Ignoring this gives a systematic error in converting \({}_1M_0\) to \({}_1q_0\).

Integration by parts
Day 5Method

A calculus technique for integrating a product of two functions, derived from the product rule for differentiation:

\[ \int_a^b u\,dv = \left[u\,v\right]_a^b - \int_a^b v\,du \]

In formal demography, integration by parts proves one of the most satisfying stable-population identities: that the crude birth rate minus the crude death rate equals the intrinsic growth rate exactly, not just approximately. Starting from the stable age distribution \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\), differentiating and integrating by parts shows that \(\int_0^\omega (-c'(x))\,dx = b - r\), so the total death rate \(d = b - r\), giving \(b - d = r\).

The same technique appears in proving the relationship between \(T_x\) and \(e_x\), and in deriving the sensitivity of demographic quantities to changes in \(r\) — making it one of the most productive tools in the formal demographer's toolkit. The trick is always choosing \(u\) and \(dv\) so that the boundary term vanishes (usually because \(c(\omega) = 0\)) and the remaining integral simplifies.

K
Kaplan-Meier estimator
Day 3Method

The standard non-parametric estimator of the survival function from a sample, allowing for right-censored observations. Rather than fixing age intervals, the curve drops at each observed event time — producing a step function:

\[ \hat{S}(t) = \prod_{t_i \leq t} \left(1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i}\right) \]

where \(d_i\) = deaths at time \(t_i\) and \(n_i\) = risk set size just before \(t_i\). Introduced by Kaplan & Meier (1958). In clinical trials the time axis is fixed and the decrement size varies; in demography it is the reverse. The KM estimator is the foundation of modern survival analysis in medicine and epidemiology.

→ Day 3, §4
Keyfitz integral (total births ever)
Day 1Method

An application of the exponential model to estimate the total number of humans ever born, pioneered by Nathan Keyfitz (1913–2010). Under exponential growth with birth rate \(b\) and growth rate \(r\), total births between times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) are:

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_2} b\,K(t)\,dt = \frac{b}{r}\left[K(t_2) - K(t_1)\right] \]

Summing across all historical eras (each with different \(K\) and \(b\)) gives approximately ~80 billion total births since the emergence of anatomically modern humans (~200,000 BCE). This means roughly 7–8% of all humans who ever lived are alive today.

Keyfitz's broader contribution was finding elegant approximations — many of them Taylor-series-based — that made demographic computation tractable before computers and kept the underlying logic visible.

→ Day 1, §10
L
Leslie matrix
Day 3Method

A square matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) used to project an age-structured population one time step forward: \(\mathbf{K}_{t+1} = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{K}_t\). The first row encodes fertility (births to each age group); the subdiagonal encodes survival probabilities (proportion surviving from one age group to the next). All other entries are zero.

\[ \mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} F_0 & F_1 & F_2 & \cdots \\ P_0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & P_1 & 0 & \cdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \end{pmatrix} \]

After many iterations, the population converges to stable age structure and grows at the dominant eigenvalue \(\lambda_1 = e^r\). Population projection is a Markov process — the future depends only on the present age structure and the vital rates, not on history.

→ Day 3, §12
Lexis diagram
Day 2Method

A two-dimensional diagram with calendar time on the horizontal axis and age on the vertical axis. Each individual life is a 45° diagonal line starting at birth (age 0, birth year) and ending at death. Three natural regions: a vertical strip = period; a horizontal strip = age group; a diagonal band = birth cohort.

Every demographic rate is a ratio: numerator = events (deaths, births) in a Lexis box; denominator = person-years of exposure in that box. The Lexis diagram resolves almost every confusion about "what is the right denominator." Introduced by Wilhelm Lexis (1875); independently developed by others — Becker (1874) and du Pâquier — but Lexis's name stuck.

→ Day 2, §2
Life table
Day 2Day 3Mortality

A table summarising the mortality experience of a cohort (or synthetic cohort) from birth to extinction. Key columns: \(l_x\) (survivors), \({}_nd_x\) (deaths), \({}_nq_x\) (death probability), \({}_np_x\) (survival probability), \({}_nL_x\) (person-years), \(T_x\) (total future person-years), \(e_x\) (life expectancy).

Period life tables apply current age-specific rates to a synthetic cohort; cohort life tables follow real people. The difference matters when mortality is improving: period \(e_0\) understates future lifespan. The first life table was constructed by John Graunt (1662); the first actuarial life table by Edmund Halley (1693) using Breslau data.

→ Day 2, §4–§8
Little's Law
Day 1Growth

In any stationary system, the average number in the system equals the arrival rate times the average sojourn time:

\[ N = \lambda \cdot \bar{W} \]

In demography: population = birth rate × life expectancy (\(K = b \cdot e_0\)). For a stationary population with \(e_0 = 80\) years and \(b = 1/80\) per year per person, \(K = 1 \times 80 = 80\) people per annual birth. Proved by John Little (1961); applies to queues, hospitals, internet routers — and populations. This is exact for stationary populations, approximate otherwise.

→ Day 1, §9
Log-linear plot
Day 1Method

A graph with the logarithm of population on the y-axis and calendar time on the x-axis. Under exponential growth \(K(t) = K_0 e^{rt}\), taking logs gives:

\[ \ln K(t) = \ln K_0 + r\cdot t \]

This is a straight line with slope \(r\). Non-linearity in the log-population plot is therefore an immediate diagnostic: a bending upward means accelerating growth; bending downward means a falling growth rate. Pre-computing the logarithm transforms a multiplicative process into an additive one — which is why the log scale is the natural one for growth problems.

→ Day 1, §12
Logistic growth
Day 1Growth

A bounded growth model where the per-capita growth rate declines linearly as the population approaches the carrying capacity \(\kappa\):

\[ \frac{dK}{dt} = rK\!\left(1 - \frac{K}{\kappa}\right) \]

The solution is an S-shaped (sigmoid) curve. At low population it resembles exponential growth; near \(\kappa\) it flattens. Growth is fastest at \(K = \kappa/2\). Proposed by Pierre-François Verhulst (1838); widely used in ecology. Real populations don't strictly follow logistic dynamics, but the qualitative S-shape is common in species introductions and technological adoption (the "diffusion of innovations" S-curve).

Lee-Carter model
Day 5MortalityMethod

The dominant approach to mortality forecasting, introduced by Ronald Lee and Lawrence Carter (1992). The model decomposes the log of the age-specific mortality rate into a time-invariant age profile plus a single time-varying mortality index:

\[ \ln m(x, t) = \alpha(x) + \beta(x)\,\kappa(t) + \varepsilon(x,t) \]

\(\alpha(x)\) captures the average age pattern of mortality; \(\kappa(t)\) is a time index summarising overall mortality level; \(\beta(x)\) describes how sensitive each age group is to changes in \(\kappa\). The parameters are estimated by singular value decomposition (SVD) — effectively a principal components decomposition of the matrix of log-rates. The index \(\kappa(t)\) is then projected forward using a random walk with drift: \(\kappa(t+1) = \kappa(t) + d + \varepsilon_t\), where \(d\) is the observed average annual decline in mortality.

The Lee-Carter model is now used by the US Social Security Administration, the UK Office for National Statistics, and many actuarial bodies for official mortality projections. Its main limitation: it assumes a single dominant trend axis (\(\beta\kappa\)), missing age-specific structural changes (e.g., the AIDS epidemic hitting prime-age adults disproportionately, or COVID-19 disproportionately affecting the very old).

Historical note: the 1992 paper was initially reviewed without a demographer among the referees at JASA. Lee and Carter had to explain why mortality forecasting mattered. It is now among the most cited papers in actuarial science and the direct ancestor of the multi-factor Li-Lee and Cairns-Blake-Dowd models used in pension risk management.

→ Day 5, §9
l(μ) — survivorship at mean age of childbearing \(l(\bar{\mu})/l(0)\)
Day 3FertilityMortality

The fraction of women in the birth cohort still alive at the mean age of childbearing \(\bar{\mu}\) (roughly 27–30 in low-mortality populations). It serves as the "mortality discount" in the key approximation:

\[ \text{NRR} \approx \text{GRR} \times \frac{l(\bar{\mu})}{l(0)} \]

This decomposition separates NRR into a pure fertility component (GRR) and a pure mortality component (\(l(\bar{\mu})/l(0)\)). A "phase plot" with GRR on one axis and \(l(\bar{\mu})\) on the other has hyperbolic NRR contours — countries can sit on the NRR = 1 replacement contour with very different combinations of fertility and mortality. In high-mortality settings (Sub-Saharan Africa), \(l(\bar{\mu}) \approx 0.80\text{–}0.85\), requiring a GRR above 1.2 to achieve replacement.

→ Day 3, §10
M
Markov process
Day 3Method

A stochastic process where the future state depends only on the present state, not on the past history of how that state was reached — the "memoryless" property. Population projection via the Leslie matrix is a deterministic Markov process: \(\mathbf{K}_{t+1} = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{K}_t\). The future age structure depends only on the current age structure and the vital rates matrix \(\mathbf{A}\).

The Markov assumption is also implicit in the standard life table: the probability of dying in \([2n, 3n)\) depends only on being alive at age \(2n\), not on how "easily" you survived to that age. This is an approximation — in reality, frail survivors carry their frailty forward. But it is a tractable and usually reasonable simplification.

→ Day 3, §12
Model life tables
Day 5MortalityMethod

Standardised sets of life tables representing the typical age patterns of mortality across a range of overall mortality levels. Used when complete empirical data are unavailable — most commonly in high-mortality countries lacking reliable vital registration. The most widely used set, the Coale-Demeny model life tables (Ansley Coale and Paul Demeny, 1966), organised 326 national life tables into four regional "families": West (most European populations), East (higher early adult mortality), North (higher old-age mortality), and South (higher infant mortality relative to child mortality).

Given a single empirical indicator — e.g., child mortality \({}_5q_0\) from a household survey — a demographer can select the appropriate family and "look up" a full life table consistent with that indicator. The Brass relational logit model is a flexible alternative that expresses any life table as a two-parameter transformation of a standard: a country's data only needs to identify \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) to pin down the full schedule.

Why they work: human mortality patterns are surprisingly constrained — at any given level of overall mortality, the relative rates by age are predictable within a narrow band. This regularity is the empirical foundation of all indirect estimation in countries without complete death registration.

Mean age of childbearing \(\bar{\mu}\)
Day 3Fertility

The weighted average age at which mothers give birth, weighting by the net maternity function \(f(x)\,l(x)/l(0)\):

\[ \bar{\mu} = \frac{\int_{15}^{50} x\,f(x)\,\frac{l(x)}{l(0)}\,dx}{\text{NRR}} \]

Approximately equal to the generation length (the average time between a woman's birth and the births of her daughters). This leads to the key rule of thumb: \(r \approx \ln(\text{NRR})\,/\,\bar{\mu}\). In low-mortality, low-fertility countries: \(\bar{\mu} \approx 28\text{–}30\) years.

Intuition: \(\bar{\mu}\) is the "centre of mass" of the NRR integrand. If NRR = 1.5 and \(\bar{\mu} = 28\), then \(r \approx \ln(1.5)/28 \approx 0.014\) — 1.4% per year.

→ Day 3, §11
N
Net maternity function \(\phi(x)\)
Day 3Fertility

The product of the age-specific fertility rate and the survivorship function, scaled by \(F_{\text{FAB}}\):

\[ \phi(x) = f(x)\cdot\frac{l(x)}{l(0)}\cdot F_{\text{FAB}} \]

The integral of \(\phi(x)\) over the reproductive ages is the NRR. It represents the expected number of daughters born in a small age interval \([x, x+dx)\) to a woman who survives. The mean of this distribution (weighted by \(\phi(x)\)) is the mean age of childbearing \(\bar{\mu}\); the NRR is its total area. This function is the bridge between the fertility schedule, the survival schedule, and the long-run growth rate.

→ Day 3, §8
Net Reproduction Ratio (NRR) NRR or \(R_0\)
Day 3FertilityMortality

The average number of daughters a woman would have over her lifetime, accounting for the probability of dying before each age of childbearing:

\[ \text{NRR} = F_{\text{FAB}} \int_{15}^{50} f(x)\,\frac{l(x)}{l(0)}\,dx \approx \text{GRR} \times l(\bar{\mu}) \]

NRR = 1 means exact replacement; NRR > 1 means long-run growth; NRR < 1 means long-run decline. This is the central formula of Day 3 — it beautifully combines fertility and mortality in a single number.

Real-world: UK ≈ 0.80 (2020); Japan ≈ 0.60; Niger ≈ 3.5. Most high-income countries are well below replacement.

Watch out: NRR < 1 does not mean the population is shrinking now — a young age structure produces momentum.

→ Day 3, §8
O
Old-age dependency ratio (OADR) OADR
Day 5Method

The ratio of the elderly population (conventionally aged 65+) to the working-age population (aged 20–64), a summary measure of the "burden" an ageing population places on the workforce:

\[ \text{OADR} = \frac{\int_{65}^{\omega} c(x)\,dx}{\int_{20}^{64} c(x)\,dx} \]

In a stable population both integrals can be computed using \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\), making OADR a function of \(r\). As \(r\) decreases (fertility falls or population ages), OADR rises — and Robert's analysis showed the sensitivity is large: a 1 percentage-point reduction in \(r\) can substantially increase the OADR over a generation.

Why it matters for policy: pay-as-you-go pension systems are essentially transfers from workers to retirees; the OADR governs the required contribution rate. Japan (2023): OADR ≈ 0.52 (roughly one worker per two pensioners). Most Sub-Saharan African countries: OADR < 0.10. The divergence is the defining fiscal challenge for high-income countries over the coming decades.

Boundary sensitivity: the choice of cutoffs (65+ vs. 60+; 15–64 vs. 20–64) affects the ratio substantially and varies across countries and contexts. Some researchers use a "prospective" definition — age at which remaining life expectancy is 15 years — rather than the fixed age of 65.

→ Day 5, §11
ODE (Ordinary Differential Equation)
Day 1Day 3Method

An equation relating a function to its own derivative. Demographic models are naturally expressed as ODEs:

\[ \underbrace{\frac{dK}{dt} = r\,K(t)}_{\text{exponential growth}}, \qquad \underbrace{\frac{dl}{dx} = -\mu(x)\,l(x)}_{\text{survival equation}} \]

Solving the exponential growth ODE by separation of variables gives \(K(t) = K_0\,e^{rt}\). Solving the survival ODE the same way gives \(l(x) = \exp(-\int_0^x \mu(y)\,dy)\). The two ODEs are structurally identical — growth is the mirror image of decay.

You do not need to solve ODEs in general to use them in demography — the key insight is that the ODE form, the explicit formula, and the log-linear form are all equivalent ways of expressing the same model. Three shirts, one body.

→ Day 1, §12 · Day 3, §1
P
Perron-Frobenius theorem
Day 4Method

A foundational result in linear algebra guaranteeing that any non-negative matrix satisfying mild irreducibility conditions has a unique largest real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1 > 0\) — the dominant eigenvalue — with a corresponding eigenvector of all-positive entries. Proved independently by Oskar Perron (1907, for positive matrices) and Georg Frobenius (1912, extended to non-negative irreducible matrices).

Applied to the Leslie matrix, Perron-Frobenius guarantees that any starting age distribution converges to the same stable age structure (the dominant right eigenvector) growing at rate \(r = \ln\lambda_1\), regardless of initial conditions. This is the mathematical explanation of why population projections "forget" their starting age composition after a few generations — a property the Day 4 demonstration with the US 1934 life table and Russian age structure made vivid.

The speed of convergence is controlled by the ratio \(|\lambda_2|/\lambda_1\): smaller ratios mean faster convergence. Sub-dominant eigenvalues produce damped oscillations — "echoes" of the starting age structure that fade over time, each with period governed by the argument of the complex eigenvalue.

Irreducibility condition: the matrix must allow "communication" between all age groups via births and survival. This requires at least one age group with positive fertility, and no break in the survival chain. Met by every realistic human population.

→ Day 4, §7
Period analysis
Day 2Method

Analysis based on a fixed calendar period, combining experience of all age groups alive at that time — a vertical slice in the Lexis diagram. Period rates describe what is happening now but refer to synthetic cohorts that do not literally exist. Contrasts with cohort analysis (following real people over time).

Period life expectancy can move rapidly with short-term shocks: COVID-19 temporarily reduced period \(e_0\) by 1–2 years in many countries in 2020–21, then partially recovered as mortality returned to trend. Cohort life expectancy is more stable but can only be computed retrospectively.

Person-years lived in interval \({}_nL_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The total person-years contributed by the cohort between exact ages \(x\) and \(x+n\):

\[ {}_nL_x = \int_x^{x+n} l(y)\,dy \approx \frac{n}{2}(l_x + l_{x+n}) \]

Geometrically: the area under the \(l(x)\) curve between \(x\) and \(x+n\). The trapezoid approximation (second form) assumes \(l(x)\) is linear within the interval — good for most age groups, less precise in infancy. \({}_nL_x\) is the denominator of the age-specific mortality rate \({}_nM_x = {}_nd_x/{}_nL_x\). Summing all \({}_nL_x\) from age \(x\) onwards gives \(T_x\).

→ Day 2, §7
Person-years (person-time)
Day 1Day 2Method

The total time lived by a group of people over an observation period. For a population of size \(K(t)\) over \([t_1, t_2]\):

\[ \text{PY} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} K(t)\,dt \approx (t_2-t_1)\cdot\frac{K(t_1)+K(t_2)}{2} \]

The conceptually correct denominator for all demographic rates. The mid-period approximation (trapezoid rule) works well when \(K(t)\) changes slowly. The area of a Lexis box equals the person-years of exposure in that box.

Population momentum
Day 3Growth

The tendency of a population to continue growing for decades after its NRR (or TFR) reaches replacement level, because of a young age structure inherited from the high-fertility past. Even if every woman immediately adopted replacement-level fertility, the population would continue to grow because there are more women now entering reproductive ages than leaving them.

Keyfitz (1971) showed that the ultimate population size \(K_\infty\) after an instantaneous drop to NRR = 1 is approximately \(K_\infty \approx K_0 \cdot e_0 \cdot b\), where current birth rates and life expectancy reflect the previous growth. For many developing countries with young age structures, momentum could add 30–50% to the eventual population even if fertility fell to replacement tomorrow.

The converse also holds: countries with aging populations (Germany, Japan) continue to shrink even when fertility temporarily rises above replacement, because the pool of women of reproductive age is contracting.

→ Day 3, §12
Population projection
Day 3Method

The mechanical application of current (or assumed future) age-specific mortality and fertility rates to an observed age structure to produce a forecast. Using the Leslie matrix: \(\mathbf{K}_{t+1} = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{K}_t\). After \(n\) steps: \(\mathbf{K}_{t+n} = \mathbf{A}^n\,\mathbf{K}_t\).

The long-run dynamics are governed by the dominant eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) of \(\mathbf{A}\): if \(\lambda_1 > 1\) the population eventually grows at rate \(r = \ln\lambda_1\); if \(\lambda_1 < 1\) it declines. Projections beyond 20–30 years are highly sensitive to fertility assumptions — a small change in TFR compounds dramatically over long horizons. UN Population Division projections include high, medium, and low variants to bracket this uncertainty.

→ Day 3, §12
Q
Quantum (fertility)
Day 3Day 5Fertility

The ultimate level of fertility — the total number of children a cohort will eventually bear, independent of the timing of those births. Contrasted with tempo, which describes when in the life course those births occur.

Cohort completed fertility is the gold-standard quantum measure: the actual average number of births per woman for a cohort that has finished its reproductive years. Period TFR conflates quantum and tempo: when women systematically postpone births (rising mean age of childbearing), the same cohort quantum generates a lower period TFR during the postponement phase — not because fewer births will eventually occur, but because births are being pushed into the future.

The Bongaarts-Feeney (1998) adjustment recovers an estimate of quantum from period data: tempo-adjusted TFR ≈ period TFR / (1 − \(\dot{\mu}\)), where \(\dot{\mu}\) is the annual rate of increase in the mean age of childbearing. Most of the dramatic TFR decline in Southern and Eastern Europe in the 1990s was subsequently shown to be a tempo distortion — cohort completed fertility fell much less than the period TFR implied. The Baby Boom of the 1950s was partly a tempo effect in reverse: women were bringing births forward, inflating period TFRs above cohort quantum.

R
Radix \(l_0\)
Day 2Mortality

The starting value of the survivorship function \(l(x)\) at age 0. By convention, life tables use either \(l_0 = 1\) (the Berkeley / probability convention used in this course) or \(l_0 = 100{,}000\) (used by most national statistics agencies). All subsequent \(l_x\) values express survival relative to the radix.

With radix = 1, every entry in the \(l_x\) column is immediately interpretable as a probability: \(l_{40} = 0.9\) means 90% of the cohort survives to age 40. With radix = 100,000, multiply by 10 to get per-1,000 figures. The columns \({}_nd_x\), \({}_nL_x\), and \(T_x\) scale with the radix; the rates \({}_nM_x\), \({}_nq_x\), and \(e_x\) are radix-independent.

Rate of natural increase \(r = b - d\)
Day 1Growth

The difference between the crude birth rate and crude death rate: \(r = b - d\). In the continuous-time model it is the instantaneous growth rate: \(K(t) = K_0 e^{rt}\). When \(r > 0\) the population grows; when \(r < 0\) it declines; when \(r = 0\) it is stationary.

The long-run intrinsic growth rate consistent with given mortality and fertility schedules can be approximated as \(r \approx \ln(\text{NRR})\,/\,\bar{\mu}\), where \(\bar{\mu}\) is the mean age of childbearing.

→ Day 1, §3 · Day 3, §10
Reproductive value \(v(x)\)
Day 5FertilityMethod

The expected future contribution of an individual aged \(x\) to the long-run growth of the population, normalised relative to a newborn. In a stable population growing at rate \(r\):

\[ v(x) = \frac{e^{rx}}{l(x)} \int_x^{\infty} e^{-ra}\,\phi(a)\,da \]

\(v(x)\) is highest in early adulthood — just before peak childbearing — because those individuals have the most high-value reproduction still ahead of them, and are already past the high mortality risk of infancy. At birth \(v(0) = 1\) by normalisation; at old age \(v(x) \to 0\) as reproductive years run out.

History: introduced by Ronald Fisher in The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection (1930), where Fisher argued that natural selection acts to maximise reproductive value, not raw current fertility. This insight connects formal demography to evolutionary biology.

In the Leslie matrix, \(v(x)\) is the left eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_1\), while the stable age distribution \(c(x)\) is the right eigenvector. Together they decompose any initial population into stable-state components and determine how much each age group "matters" for long-run population growth.

Applied use: reproductive value guides conservation prioritisation — if resources are limited, protecting pre-reproductive individuals (highest \(v\)) maximises long-run population recovery.

→ Day 5, §7
Replacement level TFR ≈ 2.1, NRR = 1
Day 3Fertility

The fertility level at which a population exactly replaces itself in the long run. In terms of NRR: replacement means NRR = 1 (exactly one daughter per woman who survives to reproduce). In terms of TFR: approximately 2.1 in low-mortality countries (slightly above 2.0 to account for the female infant mortality gap and the sex ratio at birth).

In high-mortality populations, replacement-level TFR can be 3+ because many women die before completing their reproductive years. The statement "TFR below 2.1 means population decline" is only approximately true and ignores the effect of age structure — a young population can grow even with TFR < 2.1 (momentum), and an ageing population can shrink even with TFR briefly above 2.1.

Rule of 70
Day 1Method

The mental arithmetic shortcut for doubling time: divide 70 by the percentage growth rate:

\[ T_2 \approx \frac{70}{r\%} \]

Derived from the exact formula \(T_2 = \ln(2)/r \approx 0.693/r\); since \(0.693 \approx 0.70\), "70" works for typical demographic rates (under 3%). Use "72" for higher rates — it factors nicely (72 = 2×36 = 3×24 = 4×18 = 6×12 = 8×9) and is more accurate above 6%. The same rule applies in finance (CAGR) and any other exponentially-growing quantity.

→ Day 1, §7
S
Separation of variables
Day 1Day 3Method

A technique for solving ODEs by placing all terms involving the dependent variable on one side and the independent variable on the other, then integrating both sides separately:

\[ \frac{dl}{dx} = -\mu(x)\,l \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \frac{dl}{l} = -\mu(x)\,dx \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \ln l = -\int \mu \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; l(x) = e^{-H(x)} \]

The same steps solve the growth ODE: \(dK/K = r\,dt \Rightarrow \ln K = rt + C \Rightarrow K(t) = K_0 e^{rt}\). In both cases, separation turns a differential equation into two straightforward integrals. It works whenever the ODE can be written as \(f(y)\,dy = g(x)\,dx\).

→ Day 1, §12 · Day 3, §1
Stable age distribution \(c(x)\)
Day 4Day 5Growth

The proportion of the population at exact age \(x\) in a stable population — the age structure that a population converges to after long exposure to constant vital rates. Derived by substituting the stable-state assumption \(B(t-x) = B(t)\,e^{-rx}\) into the basic accounting identity:

\[ c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x), \qquad \int_0^\omega c(x)\,dx = 1 \]

The formula has an elegant three-way interpretation: \(b\) is a normalisation constant (the birth rate); \(e^{-rx}\) discounts back to birth (faster growing populations have more younger people); \(l(x)\) removes those who died before reaching age \(x\). In the stationary case (\(r = 0\)), it simplifies to \(c(x) = l(x)/e_0\) — the age structure is purely a function of survival.

Crucially, \(c(x)\) depends on \(r\) through the exponential discount — two populations with identical life tables but different growth rates will have different age structures. A population currently below replacement can still have a young age structure (and therefore many births) because of its recent high-fertility past — this is the essence of population momentum.

\(c(x)\) is the dominant right eigenvector of the Leslie matrix, normalised to sum to 1.

→ Day 4, §9 · Day 5, §1–§2 · Day 5, §6
Stable population
Day 3Growth

A theoretical population with constant age-specific fertility and mortality rates and no migration. Such a population converges to a fixed age structure (the stable age distribution) and grows at a constant rate \(r\) — the intrinsic growth rate, determined solely by the vital rates. The stable population is the long-run equilibrium of the Leslie matrix: after enough iterations, the age composition becomes invariant to the initial structure.

A stationary population is the special case where \(r = 0\) (NRR = 1). In a stationary population, the age structure is proportional to the life table's \(l_x\) column, and Little's Law holds exactly: \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\).

→ Day 3, §12
Stationary population \(r = 0, \; b \cdot e_0 = 1\)
Day 1Growth

A population with constant size: births exactly balance deaths, \(r = 0\), NRR = 1. In a stationary population, the age structure is proportional to the life table's \(l_x\) column — the proportion at each age is determined solely by mortality, not by fertility history. Little's Law gives the elegant identity:

\[ b \cdot e_0 = 1 \qquad \Leftrightarrow \qquad \text{birth rate} = \frac{1}{\text{life expectancy}} \]

A stationary population is the reference point for many demographic calculations. No actual population is ever truly stationary, but many advanced economies (e.g., Germany, Italy, Japan) are close enough that stationary approximations are useful.

→ Day 1, §9
Stock vs. flow
Day 1Method

A stock is measured at a point in time (a snapshot): population size, number of survivors in a life table. A flow is measured over an interval: births per year, deaths per year. You cannot add a stock to a flow without specifying the time interval.

Data sources reflect this distinction: stocks are measured by censuses (every 10 years); flows are recorded continuously by vital registration systems (birth and death certificates). In countries without reliable vital registration, demographers must estimate flows from surveys or indirect methods (e.g., the Brass relational model).

→ Day 1, §2
Survival probability \({}_np_x\)
Day 2Day 3Mortality

The probability of surviving from exact age \(x\) to exact age \(x+n\):

\[ {}_np_x = \frac{l_{x+n}}{l_x} = 1 - {}_nq_x \]

Survival probabilities multiply: to survive from age 0 to age \(x = kn\), you must survive each successive \(n\)-year segment, giving \(l(x) = {}_np_0 \cdot {}_np_n \cdot {}_np_{2n} \cdots {}_np_{(k-1)n}\). This multiplicative structure (probabilities multiply; hazards add) is the key structural fact of survival analysis.

→ Day 2, §6 · Day 3, §3
Survivorship function \(l_x\) or \(l(x)\)
Day 2Mortality

The proportion of the birth cohort alive at exact age \(x\). With radix \(l_0 = 1\), it is directly the probability of surviving from birth to age \(x\). Non-increasing from 1 to 0; linked to the force of mortality by:

\[ l(x) = e^{-\int_0^x \mu(y)\,dy} = e^{-H(x)} \]

The area under the entire \(l(x)\) curve equals life expectancy at birth \(e_0\). "Rectangularisation" of the survival curve — deaths becoming more concentrated at old age — is the defining feature of the epidemiological transition. Building \(l_x\): sort ages at death for a cohort, convert to a decreasing staircase, smooth.

Synthetic cohort
Day 2Day 3Method

A hypothetical (not real) cohort that experiences the age-specific rates observed in a single calendar period as it "ages." Period life expectancy is the life expectancy of a synthetic cohort subject to today's age-specific death rates — no such cohort has ever existed.

The synthetic cohort concept is the bridge between cross-sectional (period) data and cohort interpretation. Because real cohorts cannot be observed in real time, demographers construct synthetic cohorts for current analysis and compare them to real cohorts for historical validation. Period TFR is similarly a synthetic measure — the total fertility of a woman who experiences today's age-specific fertility rates at every age.

T
Taylor series
Day 1Day 2Method

A polynomial approximation of any smooth function around a point \(a\):

\[ f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2 + \frac{f'''(a)}{3!}(x-a)^3 + \cdots \]

In formal demography, Taylor series are the engine behind every rule of thumb. Expanding \(\ln(1+r)\) around \(r = 0\) gives \(\ln(1+r) \approx r - r^2/2 + \cdots \approx r\) for small \(r\) — which explains why the slope of the log-population line equals the growth rate. Expanding \((1+r)^t\) gives \((1+r)^t \approx e^{rt}\) for small \(r\) — explaining why discrete and continuous growth models coincide at typical demographic rates. Named after Brook Taylor (1715); the special case \(a = 0\) is the Maclaurin series.

Tempo effect
Day 3Fertility

The distortion of period fertility measures (especially TFR) caused by changes in the timing of births, rather than in the ultimate number of children ("quantum"). When women postpone childbearing (increasing mean age of childbearing over time), period TFR falls below cohort TFR — not because fewer births will eventually occur, but because the same births are being pushed into the future.

Bongaarts and Feeney (1998) proposed an adjustment: tempo-adjusted TFR = period TFR / (1 − \(\dot{\mu}\)), where \(\dot{\mu}\) is the rate of change of the mean age of childbearing. Much of the dramatic TFR decline in Southern and Eastern Europe in the 1990s was a tempo effect, not a quantum decline — as demographers confirmed once those cohorts completed their fertility. The Baby Boom in the 1950s was partly a tempo effect in the opposite direction: women were bringing births forward, inflating period TFRs.

Total Fertility Rate (TFR) TFR
Day 3Fertility

The sum of age-specific fertility rates across all reproductive ages, multiplied by the age-interval width:

\[ \text{TFR} = \sum_x {}_nF_x \cdot n = \int_{15}^{50} f(x)\,dx \]

Interpreted as the average number of children a woman would have if she survived through her reproductive years and experienced current ASFRs throughout. A period measure — a synthetic snapshot, not a forecast.

Replacement level: TFR ≈ 2.1 in low-mortality countries. Real-world (2020–22): South Korea ≈ 0.78 · Japan ≈ 1.2 · UK ≈ 1.6 · USA ≈ 1.6 · France ≈ 1.8 · Niger ≈ 7.0.

Total future person-years \(T_x\)
Day 2Mortality

The total person-years lived by all survivors to age \(x\), from age \(x\) until the last member of the cohort dies:

\[ T_x = \int_x^\omega l(y)\,dy = \sum_{a \geq x} {}_nL_a \]

Geometrically: the area under the \(l(x)\) curve from \(x\) to \(\omega\). Dividing by the number alive at age \(x\) gives life expectancy: \(e_x = T_x / l_x\). At birth, \(T_0 = e_0\) (since \(l_0 = 1\)). The \(T_x\) column is computed by summing the \({}_nL_x\) column from the bottom up — the rightmost column of the standard life table layout.

→ Day 2, §8
U
UDD (Uniform Distribution of Deaths)
Day 2Mortality

The standard assumption for converting observed mortality rates \({}_nM_x\) into life table probabilities \({}_nq_x\). UDD assumes deaths are spread uniformly across the age interval, implying \({}_na_x = n/2\) (people who die do so on average halfway through the interval). This gives:

\[ {}_nq_x = \frac{n\,{}_nM_x}{1 + (n - {}_na_x)\,{}_nM_x} = \frac{n\,{}_nM_x}{1 + \frac{n}{2}\,{}_nM_x} \]

UDD is reasonable for most adult age groups but breaks down at age 0 (infant deaths are heavily front-loaded in the first weeks of life) and at the very oldest ages (above 90, mortality is so high that deaths cluster early in each interval). Alternative assumptions: Balducci (constant force, UDD on complement) and the constant-hazard assumption.

→ Day 2, §9
V
Vital registration
Day 1Method

A country's continuous administrative system for recording demographic events — births, deaths, marriages, divorces — as they occur. Provides the "flow" data (births and deaths) used in the balancing equation and for constructing period life tables.

High-quality vital registration has existed in some European countries since the 17th century (Sweden began in 1749; England and Wales from 1837). Today, it is complete or near-complete in all high-income countries and many middle-income countries, but remains absent or unreliable in much of Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia, where the majority of global deaths still go unregistered. In such settings, indirect estimation methods (Brass techniques, model life tables, sibling survival analysis) substitute for direct registration data.

Day 5: The Stable Population, Mortality Models, and Forecasting

The final day draws everything together. Day 4 ended with the Euler–Lotka equation and the mysterious formula \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\) for the stable age distribution — now we unpack that formula in full, understand why \(b - d = r\) (the fundamental decomposition of growth), and see how the shape of the age pyramid depends on the sign of \(r\). Then the course pivots to the practical toolkit of mortality modelling: Gompertz's 1820 law, Brass's relational logit model, and the celebrated Lee–Carter model — the industry standard for mortality forecasting. The day closes with a beautiful application: how sensitive is the old-age dependency ratio to the growth rate \(r\)?
⭐ Day 5 Key Takeaways
  1. The stable age distribution is \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\), where \(b = 1/\!\int e^{-rx}l(x)\,dx\) is the intrinsic birth rate. When \(r=0\) this reduces to \(b = 1/e_0\), recovering the Day 1 identity \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\).
  2. The fundamental identity \(b - d = r\) (birth rate minus death rate equals growth rate) holds exactly in the stable population, and can be proved by integration by parts.
  3. Gompertz's law (1820): adult log-mortality is approximately linear in age, \(\log \mu(x) = \log\alpha + \beta x\). This holds empirically from roughly age 35 to 85–90 across almost all historical populations.
  4. Brass's relational logit model links any life table to a standard one via a two-parameter transform of the logit of \(l(x)\). This lets demographers generate plausible life tables for data-sparse populations.
  5. The Lee–Carter model decomposes the log-mortality matrix as \(\log\,{}_nM_x(t) = a_x + k_t\,b_x\), fit by singular value decomposition. Forecasting reduces to forecasting the scalar time index \(k_t\).

✦ Clean-up from Day 4: The Full Story of \(b \cdot e_0\)

Day 5 opened with a call-back to a question Robert had posed much earlier in the week — on Day 1, when he first introduced the stationary population identity \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\). After establishing it for the stationary case (\(r = 0\)), he had asked the class: what do you think happens to \(b \cdot e_0\) when \(r \neq 0\)?

Bernice answered: "If \(r > 0\), we should expect \(b \cdot e_0 > 1\)." Robert confirmed: right intuition — and that's 95% of the story. The reasoning is straightforward: a faster-growing population has a higher birth rate \(b\) (more births relative to population), and life expectancy \(e_0\) doesn't change (it depends only on mortality), so their product should exceed 1.

💡 Intuition — Bernice's 95%

Think of \(b \cdot e_0\) as a rough measure of "how many life-years does the average birth produce, relative to what a stationary population would need?" In a growing population (\(r > 0\)), births are coming in faster than deaths, so \(b\) is large. Life expectancy \(e_0\) is unchanged. So \(b \cdot e_0 > 1\). In a shrinking population (\(r < 0\)), births are slow, \(b\) is small, and \(b \cdot e_0 < 1\). This is exactly the right intuition.

The Remaining 5%: The Age Distribution Changes Too

After deriving the Euler–Lotka equation on Day 4, the full picture becomes clear. Recall that the birth rate in the stable population is:

Intrinsic birth rate in the stable population
\[b = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx}\]

So \(\dfrac{1}{b} = \int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx\), and therefore:

The product b · e₀ when r ≠ 0
\[b \cdot e_0 = \frac{e_0}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega l(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx}\]

When \(r = 0\): the \(e^{-rx}\) factor is 1 everywhere, so numerator and denominator are equal, giving \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\). ✓

When \(r > 0\): the factor \(e^{-rx} < 1\) for all \(x > 0\), so the denominator is smaller than the numerator, giving \(b \cdot e_0 > 1\). ✓ Bernice was right.

When \(r < 0\): the factor \(e^{-rx} > 1\) for all \(x > 0\), so the denominator is larger, giving \(b \cdot e_0 < 1\).

The other 5% is the reason the product isn't just \(b\) scaling up or down: the denominator \(\int e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx\) is not simply \(e_0\) rescaled — it is a different weighted integral, one that weights older ages less (if \(r > 0\)) or more (if \(r < 0\)). In other words, the age distribution \(c(x)\) changes with \(r\), and this is precisely what the rest of today's lecture unpacks. The identity \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\) is a special case of a richer family of relationships parameterised by \(r\).

📝 Note — A Consistency Check

You can verify the formula immediately for the US 1934 cohort from Day 4. We found \(r = 0.0157\,\text{yr}^{-1}\) and \(e_0 \approx 62\,\text{yr}\). Since \(r > 0\), we expect \(b > 1/e_0 \approx 0.0161\,\text{yr}^{-1}\). Indeed, the intrinsic birth rate works out to slightly above this — the growing population has a slightly elevated birth rate relative to the stationary baseline.

1 The Stable Age Distribution \(c(x)\): Deriving the Formula

With the clean-up complete, Robert moved to the main business of the day: unpacking the formula that was written down at the end of Day 4 without full explanation. In the stable state, Robert had shown that \(K(x,t) = B(t-x)\,l(x)\) — the number of women aged \(x\) at time \(t\) equals the number of births \(x\) years ago, scaled by the survival probability. And in the stable state, births grow exponentially: \(B(t-x) = B(t)\,e^{-rx}\). Substituting:

Women of age x at time t in the stable population
\[K(x,t) = B(t)\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\]

Now divide both sides by \(K(t)\), the total number of women at time \(t\):

Stable age distribution — proportion aged x
\[ c(x) \;=\; \frac{K(x,t)}{K(t)} \;=\; \frac{B(t)\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)}{K(t)} \;=\; b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x) \]

where $b = B(t)/K(t)$ is simply the birth rate — the ratio of births to total population. This is the formula for the stable age distribution.

📖 Definition — Stable Age Distribution

In a stable population growing at rate \(r\), the proportion of the population aged exactly \(x\) is: \[c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\] where \(b\) is the intrinsic birth rate and \(l(x)\) is the survival function. The discrete 5-year-group analogue is: \[{}_nC_x \;=\; b \cdot {}_nL_x \cdot e^{-rx}\]

💡 Intuition — Why This Formula Makes Sense

Think of the stable population as a machine continuously minting new births at rate \(b\). A birth cohort born \(x\) years ago has been shrunk in two ways: (1) by the factor \(e^{-rx}\), because the total population was smaller then (if \(r>0\)) so there were fewer births, and (2) by the factor \(l(x)\), because only a fraction \(l(x)\) of those births survived to age \(x\). The product \(e^{-rx}\,l(x)\) is the "effective size" of each cohort, and \(b\) is just the normalising constant to make it all integrate to 1.

Finding the Constant \(b\)

Since \(c(x)\) is a probability density — it must integrate to 1 over all ages — we can find \(b\) immediately:

Normalization of c(x)
\[ \int_0^\omega c(x)\,dx = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; b\!\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \boxed{b = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx}} \]

The Special Case \(r = 0\): The Stationary Population

When \(r = 0\), the exponential factor disappears: \(e^{-rx} = 1\) for all \(x\). So:

Birth rate in the stationary population (r = 0)
\[b = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega l(x)\,dx} = \frac{1}{e_0}\]

because \(\int_0^\omega l(x)\,dx = e_0\), life expectancy at birth (this is the standard formula for life expectancy, which you derived on Day 2). So at \(r=0\): \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\) — exactly the identity from Day 1. The stationary population is just a stable population with \(r = 0\).

📝 Note — Why "Stationary" and "Stable" are Different Words

A stationary population has \(r = 0\): it neither grows nor shrinks. Its age distribution is proportional to \(l(x)\). A stable population has a constant growth rate \(r\) (which may be positive, negative, or zero) and a fixed age distribution \(c(x)\). Every stationary population is stable (with \(r=0\)), but not every stable population is stationary. The term "stable" refers to the age structure being fixed, not to the size.

⚠ Common Mistake — Confusing l(x) and c(x)

\(l(x)\) and \(c(x)\) are completely different functions. \(l(x)\) is the survival function from your life table — it depends only on age-specific mortality, not on fertility or growth. \(c(x)\) is the stable age distribution — it also depends on the growth rate \(r\). If \(r > 0\), then \(c(x) < l(x)\) (rescaled) at older ages because the exponential discount \(e^{-rx}\) down-weights older cohorts. Only when \(r=0\) does the stable age distribution become proportional to \(l(x)\).

2 The Birth Rate \(b\) as a Weighted Mean of Fertility

There's a beautiful way to interpret the birth rate \(b\) in the stable population. In any population, the birth rate is births per person: \(b = \int f(x)\,c(x)\,dx / \int c(x)\,dx\). Since \(\int c(x)\,dx = 1\), this simplifies to a weighted mean of age-specific fertility rates, with the age distribution \(c(x)\) as weights:

Birth rate as weighted mean fertility in the stable population
\[ b = \frac{\displaystyle\int f(x)\,c(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int c(x)\,dx} = \int f(x)\,c(x)\,dx \]

Substituting \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\):

Expanding b using the stable age distribution
\[ b = \int f(x)\cdot b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx = b\int f(x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx \]

Dividing both sides by \(b\):

The Euler–Lotka equation (once more!)
\[1 = \int_0^\omega f(x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx\]

We've re-derived the Euler–Lotka equation — confirming our machinery is consistent. The birth rate \(b\) cancels out, leaving the equation that implicitly defines \(r\) in terms of the fertility and mortality schedules.

3 The Fundamental Identity: \(b - d = r\) via Integration by Parts

One of the most satisfying results in formal demography is that in the stable population, the birth rate minus the death rate equals the growth rate — exactly, not approximately. This is the stable-population generalisation of the balancing equation from Day 1. The proof uses integration by parts.

A Five-Minute Reminder: Integration by Parts

Integration by parts is the integral analogue of the product rule for derivatives. If \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\), then integrating both sides gives \(\int f'g = fg - \int fg'\). This is written compactly:

Integration by parts
\[\int f'\,g = f\,g - \int f\,g'\]
💡 Intuition — When to Use Integration by Parts

Use it when you have a product inside an integral and one of the factors becomes simpler when differentiated while the other is easy to integrate. Here, we'll apply it to an integral involving \(e^{-rx}\,l(x)\), choosing \(f' = -r\,e^{-rx}\) (so \(f = e^{-rx}\)) and \(g = l(x)\) (whose derivative is \(-\mu(x)\,l(x)\), the hazard rate times the survival function, which you derived on Day 3).

Death Rate in the Stable Population

The death rate in the stable population is the weighted average of the hazard rate \(\mu(x)\), weighted by the stable age distribution:

Death rate d in the stable population
\[d = \int_0^\omega \mu(x)\,c(x)\,dx = \int_0^\omega \mu(x)\,b\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = b\int_0^\omega \mu(x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx\]

Proof that \(b - d = r\)

Proof — b − d = r
Start from the normalization: \(\displaystyle b\int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = 1\), so \(\displaystyle\int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = \frac{1}{b}\).
Differentiate with respect to \(r\): \(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dr}\int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = \int_0^\omega (-x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx\). But this is just the mean age of the stable age distribution, with a sign flip.
Apply integration by parts to \(\displaystyle\int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx\). Set \(f' = -r\,e^{-rx}\) (so \(f = e^{-rx}\)) and \(g = l(x)/(-r)\)... actually use the known result \(l'(x) = -\mu(x)\,l(x)\).
Let \(u = e^{-rx}\), \(dv = dl(x)\). Then \(du = -r\,e^{-rx}\,dx\), \(v = l(x)\). Integration by parts gives: \[\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,dl(x) = \left[e^{-rx}\,l(x)\right]_0^\omega + r\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,dx\] The boundary term: at \(x=0\), \(l(0)=1\), \(e^0=1\); at \(x=\omega\), \(l(\omega)=0\). So the boundary term \(= -1\).
The left side: \(dl(x) = -\mu(x)\,l(x)\,dx\), so the left side is \(-\int_0^\omega \mu(x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = -d/b\).
Putting it together: \(-d/b = -1 + r/b\), multiply through by \(b\): \(-d = -b + r\), therefore \(\boxed{b - d = r}\).  ∎

This is genuinely beautiful: the continuous stable-population arithmetic works out perfectly. The messy integral version of "births minus deaths equals growth" is exact, not just a first-order approximation.

🌍 Demography Note — What b − d = r Actually Means

In real populations, the balancing equation is approximate because of migration and because the population is not truly stable. But in a stable closed population (fixed rates, no migration), it is exact. This is why demographers who work with model stable populations can treat \(r = b - d\) as exact — they're by construction working in a closed, fixed-rates setting. For actual national populations, especially ones with migration, the identity holds only approximately.

5 Shapes of the Stable Age Pyramid

The stable age distribution formula \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\) tells us exactly how the shape of the age pyramid depends on \(r\). There are three qualitatively different cases, each with a different visual signature.

Case 1: \(r = 0\) — The Stationary Population

When \(r = 0\), the formula becomes \(c(x) \propto l(x)\). The age distribution is simply the survival curve laid on its side. Because survival is monotonically decreasing — more people alive at young ages, fewer at old — the resulting "pyramid" is actually a rectangle that tapers slightly at the top (the classic stationary population shape). Robert noted: this is why some people refer to \({}_nL_x\) as "the stationary population age distribution" — it literally is, when \(r=0\).

Case 2: \(r > 0\) — A Growing Population

The factor \(e^{-rx}\) shrinks each cohort's contribution as age increases. For a growing population (\(r > 0\)), older cohorts were born when the population was smaller, so they were smaller cohorts to begin with. The age pyramid narrows steeply as you go up — a classic young, growing-population pyramid shape.

Case 3: \(r < 0\) — A Declining Population

Now \(e^{-rx} = e^{|r|x}\) increases with age: older cohorts were born when the population was larger. At young ages this effect is small, but at very old ages \(l(x)\) pulls things back down (there are few survivors at 90+). The resulting shape bulges in the middle and bends back at the top — Robert called it a beehive. This is the shape you see in countries with sustained below-replacement fertility.

🏛 Historical Note — Population Pyramids

The "population pyramid" as a visual tool was popularised in the late 19th century by statistical offices trying to display census data. The classic triangular shape — wide base, narrow top — reflects a high-fertility, high-mortality (and growing) population. Today, many wealthy countries have "urns" or "barrels" rather than pyramids: wide in the middle (the baby boom generation) and narrowing at both ends. The Brass relational logit model and Lee–Carter forecasts, both covered in this lecture, were developed precisely to handle the variety of these shapes across countries and time.

⚠ Common Mistake — The "Beehive" Shape is Counterintuitive

When students first encounter \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\) for \(r < 0\), they sometimes think: "if the population is shrinking, shouldn't there be fewer young people?" Yes — and that's exactly what happens at very young ages. But the oldest living cohorts (say, 60–75 year-olds) were born when the population was large (before fertility collapsed), so there are many of them relative to the now-smaller young cohorts. At very old ages (\(x > 80\)), \(l(x)\) shrinks rapidly and overrides the \(e^{+|r|x}\) growth. The result: a bulge in the middle, a thin base, and a thin top.

6 Eigendecomposition of the Leslie Matrix

Robert returned to the Leslie matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) to give a more complete picture of what the eigenvalues and eigenvectors mean. Day 4 already showed that the largest real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) determines the long-run growth rate. Now we can understand the full structure.

The Eigendecomposition \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{U}^{-1}\)

Any diagonalisable matrix can be written as \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{U}^{-1}\), where \(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\) is diagonal with eigenvalues along the diagonal, and \(\mathbf{U}\) is the matrix of eigenvectors. For the Leslie matrix, \(\mathbf{U}\) is orthonormal.

The power of this decomposition is that it makes iterated projection trivial:

t-step projection via eigendecomposition
\[ \mathbf{A}^t = \mathbf{U}\,\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^t\,\mathbf{U}^{-1} \qquad\text{where}\qquad \boldsymbol{\Lambda}^t = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1^t & & \\ & \lambda_2^t & \\ & & \ddots\end{pmatrix} \]

What Each Eigenvalue Does

The Leslie matrix has as many eigenvalues as age groups. The largest real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) gives the long-run growth rate per projection step \(n\):

Intrinsic growth rate from the leading eigenvalue
\[\lambda_1 = e^{r \cdot n} \;\Longrightarrow\; r = \frac{\log \lambda_1}{n}\]

For 5-year projection steps (\(n=5\)): \(\lambda_1 = e^{5r}\), so \(r = \log(\lambda_1)/5\). The complex eigenvalues describe oscillations around the stable path: they look like \(e^{i\pi t}\) terms (recall \(e^{i\pi} = -1\), Euler's identity), producing damped sinusoidal "echoes" as the population converges to its stable shape.

What the Eigenvectors Tell You

The right eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_1\) (the first column of \(\mathbf{U}\)) gives the stable age distribution: it is proportional to \(c(x) = b\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\). So the Leslie matrix eigendecomposition is a compact summary of everything: the life table \({}_nL_x\) and the fertility \({}_nF_x\) are embedded in \(\mathbf{A}\), and their combined effect on long-run growth and structure comes out of the eigendecomposition.

The left eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_1\) is related to a concept called reproductive value — how much does one extra person of age \(x\) contribute to the long-run population? A newborn baby contributes reproductive value 1 by convention; a woman aged 25 (prime reproductive years) contributes more; an elderly person contributes less. Robert mentioned this in passing as something the day's format didn't have time to develop fully.

🏛 Historical Note — Fisher's Reproductive Value

The concept of reproductive value was introduced by R. A. Fisher in his 1930 The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection — yes, the same Fisher who gave us the F-statistic, maximum likelihood, and analysis of variance. Fisher defined it as the present "value" of an individual of age \(x\) in terms of future offspring, discounted at rate \(r\). It appears naturally as the left eigenvector of the Leslie matrix. The ecological application — how to allocate conservation effort across age classes — was one of the motivations for Caswell's matrix population models in the 1980s.

7 Gompertz's Law of Mortality (1820)

The second half of Day 5 pivoted from the theory of stable populations to the practical toolkit of mortality modelling — how demographers build and forecast the life tables that feed into all the formulae we've seen. The natural starting point is the oldest empirical regularity in human mortality.

Benjamin Gompertz, an actuary at the Guardian Life Insurance Company, observed in 1820 that the force of mortality (hazard rate) increases exponentially with age, at least for adults:

Gompertz's Law (1820)
\[ \mu(x) = \alpha\,e^{\beta x} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \log\mu(x) = \log\alpha + \beta x \]

In other words, log-mortality is linear in age. The parameter \(\alpha\) sets the overall level (the y-intercept on the log scale) and \(\beta\) determines how steeply mortality rises with age.

🏛 Historical Note — Benjamin Gompertz (1779–1865)

Gompertz was a self-taught mathematician of Portuguese-Jewish heritage. Barred from English universities because of his religion (before Jewish emancipation), he taught himself mathematics and was eventually elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1819. His mortality law appeared in a paper in the Philosophical Transactions in 1825. It remains, two centuries later, the most widely used parametric mortality model in actuarial science. Makeham (1860) later added a constant age-independent hazard: \(\mu(x) = A + \alpha\,e^{\beta x}\) — the Makeham correction captures background non-senescent mortality (accidents, infectious disease) that Gompertz's pure exponential misses at young ages.

🌍 Demography Note — Where Gompertz Works (and Doesn't)

Empirically, Gompertz holds remarkably well for adults aged roughly 35 to 85–90, across almost all historical populations Robert examined. Below 35, the curve is non-monotone: child mortality falls through the teens, then rises in the 20s (accidents, violence) before settling into the adult Gompertz regime. Above ~90, mortality appears to decelerate relative to the Gompertz extrapolation — this is the subject of ongoing debate ("late-life mortality plateaus"). Robert showed French and Swedish data confirming linearity of \(\log m_x\) in the adult age range for multiple historical periods.

The Gompertz Scatter: Sweden 1751–1910

Robert showed a scatter plot of the two Gompertz parameters \((\log\alpha, \beta)\) estimated for Swedish birth cohorts from 1751 to 1910. Each point in the scatter is one cohort, estimated by fitting a line to \(\log m_x\) vs age 40–85. The key observation: as time progresses, mortality improves — but the points trace out a curved path in \((\log\alpha, \beta)\) space, not a straight line. This means the improvements are not simply parallel shifts of the log-mortality line; both the level and the slope change. This structure motivated more flexible models — starting with Brass's relational approach.

8 Brass's Relational Logit Model for Life Tables

In the early 1970s, William Brass at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine asked: can we model the full survival curve \(l(x)\) simply, taking advantage of its monotone shape? The key insight comes from two pro tips Robert quoted directly:

  1. It is easier to model things that are monotone than curves that go up and down.
  2. It is easier to model things that are straight than things that are curvy.

The survival function \(l(x)\) is monotonically decreasing from 1 to 0 — nice property (1). Brass looked for a transformation \(f(l_x)\) that would make it straighter. His answer: the logit transform.

Brass's logit transformation of l(x)
\[ f(l_x) = \log\frac{l_x}{1 - l_x} = \text{logit}(l_x) \]
💡 Intuition — Why the Logit?

The logit is the standard transformation for mapping a probability (0 to 1) to the whole real line (−∞ to +∞). For \(l_x\) close to 1 (young ages), \(\text{logit}(l_x)\) is a large positive number. For \(l_x\) close to 0 (oldest ages), it's a large negative number. Crucially, the logit transformation stretches out the endpoints — where small changes in \(l_x\) are squished — making the overall curve more linear. Brass found empirically that when you plot \(\text{logit}(l_x)\) against \(\text{logit}(l_x^{\text{std}})\) for a standard life table, the relationship is approximately linear across a wide range of populations.

The Relational Model

Given a standard life table \(l_x^{\text{std}}\), Brass's model says any other life table \(l_x^{\text{new}}\) can be approximated by a two-parameter linear relationship on the logit scale:

Brass relational logit model
\[ \alpha + \beta\,f(l_x^{\text{std}}) = f(l_x^{\text{new}}) \qquad\text{where}\qquad f(\cdot) = \text{logit}(\cdot) \]

Inverting: \(l_x^{\text{new}} = f^{-1}\!\left(\alpha + \beta\,f(l_x^{\text{std}})\right)\), i.e., apply the logistic function to the shifted-and-scaled logit of the standard. The two parameters have clear interpretations:

  • \(\alpha\) controls the level of mortality — shifting the logit curve up or down corresponds to a higher or lower overall mortality regime.
  • \(\beta\) controls the shape of the age distribution of mortality — how fast the gradient of survival changes with age. \(\beta = 1\) means the shape is the same as the standard; \(\beta \neq 1\) tilts the logit curve.
🏛 Historical Note — William Brass and Model Life Tables

William Brass (1921–1999) spent most of his career at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine, where he developed a range of indirect estimation methods for populations with incomplete data — particularly sub-Saharan Africa, where vital registration was sparse. The relational logit model was part of a broader toolkit for "making the most of bad data." The Coale–Demeny regional model life tables (published 1966, extended 1983) provided the standard reference tables that Brass's model could be applied to. These ~300 life tables, mostly from historical Europe (split into North, South, East, West families), remain in use today. The UN's own model life tables extend this to include non-European populations.

9 The Lee–Carter Model: Forecasting Mortality

The Brass model is great for cross-sectional comparisons, but how do you forecast future mortality? This is the domain of the Lee–Carter model, published by Ronald Lee and Lawrence Carter in the Journal of the American Statistical Association in 1992, and now the dominant approach in official mortality forecasting worldwide.

The Model

The central idea is to factorise the log of the age-specific death rate matrix into an age component and a time component:

Lee–Carter model (1992)
\[ \log\,{}_nM_x(t) = a_x + k_t\,b_x \]

where:

  • \(a_x\) is the baseline log-mortality at each age, averaged over time.
  • \(k_t\) is a time index — a single number per year that captures the overall level of mortality improvement.
  • \(b_x\) is the age sensitivity pattern — which age groups improve fastest when \(k_t\) changes.
💡 Intuition — The Log-Mortality Matrix

Think of arranging all your mortality data in a matrix: rows are age groups (19 of them, from 0 to 85+), columns are years (55 years, 1933–1987 in the US example Robert used). Each cell contains \(\log {}_nM_x(t)\). The Lee–Carter model says this entire matrix can be approximated as the sum of a row-vector \(a_x\) (the same for all years) plus the outer product of two vectors: \(b_x\) (the age pattern) and \(k_t\) (the time trend). It's a rank-1 approximation to the centered log-mortality matrix — like a demographic principal components analysis.

Fitting via Singular Value Decomposition

You cannot do an eigendecomposition on a non-square matrix. Instead, you use Singular Value Decomposition (SVD):

SVD of the centred log-mortality matrix
\[ \text{SVD}\!\left(\log {}_nM_x - a_x\right) = \mathbf{U}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\,\mathbf{V}^* \]

The first singular vectors give the Lee–Carter components: \(b_x\) is derived from the first column of \(\mathbf{U}\) (the "left singular vector"), and \(k_t\) is derived from the first column of \(\mathbf{V}\) scaled by the first singular value. Both are rescaled so that \(\sum_x b_x = 1\) (identifying constraint). The SVD finds the best rank-1 approximation to the matrix in the least-squares sense.

📝 Note — Robert's R Code for Lee–Carter

Robert provided working R code for this analysis using US mortality data (HMD, 1933–1987). The key steps: (1) read the death rate matrix, (2) take logs, (3) subtract row means to get \(a_x\), (4) call svd(), (5) rescale the first singular vectors to get \(b_x\) and \(k_t\). The resulting \(k_t\) series is then forecast using an ARIMA model (usually a random walk with drift), and the forecast \(\hat{k}_{t+h}\) is plugged back into \(\log M_x(t+h) = a_x + \hat{k}_{t+h}\,b_x\) to get future mortality schedules. Robert's comment: "It is stunning how well this works."

🏛 Historical Note — Lee–Carter and Its Legacy

Ronald Lee (UC Berkeley, Robert Chung's colleague) and Lawrence Carter published their landmark paper in 1992. It became one of the most-cited papers in demography. The US Social Security Administration adopted a version of it for official projections. The original paper estimated that US female life expectancy at birth would reach around 86 years by 2065 — a projection that has so far proved roughly accurate. Critiques of the model centre on its assumption of a single uniform improvement pattern \(b_x\) across all ages and times — in practice, recent improvements have been concentrated at older ages in wealthy countries, and at young ages in developing countries. Many extensions exist (LC with cointegration, coherent multi-population models, etc.), but the basic SVD structure remains at the core of most.

10 Below-Replacement Fertility: Why Robert Isn't Worried (Yet)

Robert posed a question to the class: Why am I not very worried about below-replacement fertility? He answered it using the tools from the course.

The formula \(r = \log(\text{NRR})/G\) shows that \(r\) depends not just on the NRR but on the generation time \(G\). As fertility falls and the population ages, \(G\) tends to rise (women have children later). An increasing \(G\) moderates the decline in \(r\). This is a buffer effect: fertility falling towards replacement doesn't immediately produce rapid population decline.

Robert then raised the China example as a worked question:

📝 Note — China's Population and Below-Replacement Fertility

China has had below-replacement fertility since approximately 1979 — that is, for about 46 years by the time of this lecture (2026). The question: how much lower is China's population today than it was in 1979? Using the exponential growth model: if \(r < 0\), the population should be shrinking exponentially. But China's population in 2026 is actually larger than in 1979 (roughly 1.4 billion vs 970 million in 1979). Why? Because the population had huge momentum — a large base of young women of childbearing age in 1979, born during the high-fertility era. Even with below-replacement fertility, there are enough women in reproductive ages to keep producing more births than deaths for several decades. The population only began to decline around 2022–2023. This is population momentum: the demographic structure "remembers" past fertility long after the rates have changed.

🌍 Real World — The Dependency Ratio Debate

The chart on page 9 of Robert's notes shows China's population growing from 2000 to a peak around 2022, then beginning to decline. The area under the curve between 2000 and 2026 represents total person-years lived during below-replacement fertility — far more than a naive calculation of "we've had r < 0 for 46 years" would suggest. This is why demographers are more nuanced than newspaper headlines about "population collapse."

11 Old-Age Dependency and the Growth Rate \(r\)

The final topic of the course was a beautiful application of the stable population calculus. Many countries worry about the old-age dependency ratio — the ratio of elderly people to working-age adults. Robert showed how to calculate exactly how sensitive this ratio is to the intrinsic growth rate \(r\).

Defining the Old-Age Dependency Ratio

In the stable population, using the stable age distribution \(c(x) = b\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\):

Old-age dependency ratio in the stable population
\[ \alpha = \frac{{}_\omega C_{65}}{{_{46}}C_{18}} = \frac{\text{pop age 65+}}{\text{pop age 18--64}} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_{65}^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_{18}^{64} l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx} \]

How Does \(\alpha\) Change with \(r\)?

To find \(d\alpha/dr\), take the logarithmic derivative (easier than differentiating \(\alpha\) directly):

Log-differentiating the dependency ratio
\[ \log\alpha = \log\!\int_{65}^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx \;-\; \log\!\int_{18}^{64} l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx \]

Differentiating each term with respect to \(r\) (using \(\frac{d}{dr}\int_{a}^{b} l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx = \int_a^b (-x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx\)):

Derivative of log(α) with respect to r
\[ \frac{d}{dr}\log\alpha \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\int_{65}^\omega (-x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_{65}^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx} \;-\; \frac{\displaystyle\int_{18}^{64} (-x)\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_{18}^{64} l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx} \]

Each fraction is a weighted mean age within its age range — the mean age of the retired population, and the mean age of the working-age population, respectively. Note the minus signs:

Sensitivity formula — elegant result
\[ \frac{d\log\alpha}{dr} \approx -\bar{x}_{65+} + \bar{x}_{18\text{--}64} \approx -75 + 40 = -35 \]

So \(d\alpha/\alpha \approx -35 \cdot dr\), meaning: a 1 percentage-point decrease in \(r\) leads to approximately a 35% increase in the old-age dependency ratio. This is why low (or negative) population growth is so alarming to pension systems and health services.

💡 Intuition — Why the Number −35?

The difference \(-\bar{x}_{65+} + \bar{x}_{18\text{--}64} \approx -75 + 40 = -35\) is the negative of the gap between the mean age of retired people (~75) and the mean age of working-age people (~40). The bigger this gap, the more sensitive the dependency ratio is to changes in \(r\). This makes intuitive sense: a lower growth rate disproportionately shrinks the young, so it inflates the old relative to working-age. The 35-year gap between the "average retiree" and the "average worker" is what amplifies small changes in \(r\) into large swings in the dependency ratio.

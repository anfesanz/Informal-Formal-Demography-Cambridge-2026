Day 4: The Leslie Matrix, the Renewal Equation, and the Euler–Lotka Equation

Day 4 is the day all the threads come together. We have mortality (the life table), we have fertility (TFR, GRR, NRR), and now we need a machine that can take an actual population with a real age structure and project it forward in time. That machine is the Leslie matrix — a piece of linear algebra that turns population projection into a single matrix multiplication. The day opens with a cleanup of the mean age of fertility from Day 3, shows some useful rules of thumb, then builds up the projection framework step by step. It closes with arguably the most important equation in all of formal demography: the Euler–Lotka equation, which unifies births, survival, and growth into a single expression and reveals the properties of a stable population.
⭐ Day 4 Key Takeaways
  1. The net maternity function \(\phi(x) = f(x)\,l(x)\) combines fertility and survival; its mean is the mean age of childbearing weighted by survival.
  2. Population projection is just repeated matrix multiplication: \(\mathbf{K}(t+1) = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{K}(t)\). The Leslie matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) encodes the life table (survivorship on the sub-diagonal) and fertility (on the top row).
  3. Under fixed rates, any starting population will converge to a stable age distribution and a constant intrinsic growth rate \(r\). This follows from the Perron–Frobenius theorem. In the lecture demonstration: \(r \approx 0.0157\,\text{yr}^{-1}\).
  4. The Euler–Lotka equation \(1 = \int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx\) is derived by substituting the stable-state birth series into the renewal equation. It is "the most important equation in all of formal demography."
  5. A stationary population is simply the special case of a stable population where \(r = 0\). Setting \(r=0\) in the Euler–Lotka equation immediately gives the Day 1 identity \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\).

1 Clean-Up from Day 3: Mean Age of Fertility and the Net Maternity Function

Day 4 began — as every day in the course does — with a short "clean-up" from the previous session. Day 3 had introduced the mean age of childbearing \(\mu\) without quite finishing the story. Two versions of the mean age were distinguished, and the discrete approximations used in practice were tidied up.

The Two Versions of the Mean Age

The simplest mean age is just the average of the fertility schedule \(f(x)\), weighted by \(f(x)\) itself — essentially asking "what is the average age at which births happen, ignoring mortality?"

Mean age of fertility — ignoring mortality (continuous version)
\[ \bar{m} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\int x\,f(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int f(x)\,dx} \]

But for a real cohort of women, not all of them survive to every age of childbearing. A woman who dies at 25 cannot have children at 30. The demographic mean age — the mean age at which actual births occur in the cohort — should be weighted by both fertility and survival:

Mean age of childbearing — weighted by survival (continuous version)
\[ \mu \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\int x\,f(x)\,l(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int f(x)\,l(x)\,dx} \]
📖 Definition — Net Maternity Function

The product \(\phi(x) = f(x)\,l(x)\) is called the net maternity function. It gives the expected number of (female) births per woman still alive at age \(x\), weighted by the probability of surviving to that age from birth. Integrating it gives the NRR: \[\text{NRR} = F_{\text{FAB}} \int \phi(x)\,dx = F_{\text{FAB}} \int f(x)\,l(x)\,dx\] The mean \(\mu\) is therefore the mean of this curve — the centre of mass of the net maternity function.

The discrete version used in practice replaces \(f(x)\,l(x)\) with \({}_nF_x \cdot {}_nL_x\), where \({}_nF_x\) is the age-specific fertility rate and \({}_nL_x\) is the life-table person-years lived.

Discrete Versions for Computation

In practice with 5-year age groups (so \(n = 5\)) and age-group midpoints \(x + n/2\):

Discrete mean age formulas (5-year age groups)
\[ \bar{m} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_x \!\left(x + \tfrac{n}{2}\right) {}_nF_x}{\displaystyle\sum_x {}_nF_x} \qquad\quad \mu \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_x \!\left(x + \tfrac{n}{2}\right) {}_nF_x \cdot {}_nL_x}{\displaystyle\sum_x {}_nF_x \cdot {}_nL_x} \]

The \(F_{\text{FAB}}/n\) factors in the numerator and denominator cancel when computing the ratio, so they don't affect \(\mu\) itself.

📐 Higher Moments — Variance of Childbearing Age

The same approach generalises to higher moments. The variance of the childbearing age distribution uses \((x + n/2)^2\) as the weight instead of \((x + n/2)\), and the skewness uses \((x + n/2)^3\). These matter for understanding whether a fertility schedule is early-skewed (lots of teenage births) or late-skewed (births concentrated at 30+). The mean alone doesn't tell you the whole shape.

2 Cocktail Party Rules of Thumb for \(\mu\) and \(r\)

The lecture made an empirical observation that is useful for back-of-envelope calculations. For almost every national-level population in the world, the mean age of childbearing \(\mu\) falls within a surprisingly narrow range:

\(\mu \in [\approx 25,\; \approx 39]\) for virtually all national populations — typically around 28–30.

Some specific examples from the lecture: East Asia (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan) has a very late mean age, around \(\mu \approx 33\), reflecting the extremely delayed childbearing in these countries. The USA in 1934 had an unusually early mean age, around \(\mu \approx 26\), reflecting the Depression-era pattern of early marriage and high fertility in young women.

Using \(r \approx \ln(\text{NRR})/\mu\) from Day 3, and plugging in \(\mu \approx 28\) as a universal approximation:

💡 Intuition — The Cocktail Party Rules of Thumb

With \(\mu \approx 28\) as a universal approximation, we get three quick-and-dirty formulas that you can use at a party to impress demographers (or bore non-demographers):

\[\text{NRR} \approx e^{28r} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad r \approx \frac{\ln(\text{NRR})}{28} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \frac{\ln(\text{NRR})}{28} \approx r\]

A more memorable form: \(r \approx \dfrac{\log_e \text{NRR}}{28}\). Since \(\log_e \text{NRR} \approx \text{NRR}-1\) for NRR near 1 (first-order Taylor expansion), an even rougher approximation is \(r \approx (\text{NRR}-1)/28\).

3 England & Wales Life Tables: 1841 vs 1930 Cohorts

The lecture briefly showed graphs comparing cohort life tables for England and Wales (both sexes combined) for two birth cohorts: those born in 1841 and those born in 1930. These are 5-year abridged tables from the Human Mortality Database. The comparison is striking.

📐 Cohort vs Period Life Tables

A cohort life table follows an actual group of people born in the same year and records how they actually died over their lifetimes. A period life table takes age-specific death rates from a single calendar year and asks "what would happen to a hypothetical cohort?" The England & Wales tables shown here are cohort tables — so the 1841 table really does track people born in 1841, most of whom would have died by around 1920.

🌍 Real World — What Changed Between 1841 and 1930?

The dramatic difference between the 1841 and 1930 cohorts is almost entirely driven by improvements in infant and child mortality in the early portion of life. Improvements in sanitation (clean water, sewage systems), nutrition, and eventually medical care (vaccines, antibiotics) radically reduced the number of children dying before age 5. Life expectancy at birth rose from around 41 years (1841 cohort) to around 63 years (1930 cohort) — a gain of 22 years in less than a century. Note that life expectancy at age 65 improved much less — most of the gain was at the young end.

5 The Population Projection Framework: Thinking About the Arrows

To project a population forward, we need to track what happens to each age group over one time step. The lecture introduced the framework using a diagram of boxes (age groups) connected by arrows (transitions).

The One-Sex Model and Its Assumptions

The model is deliberately simplified:

  • One sex — we track only females (or only males). This avoids the complication of how male and female populations interact in producing births.
  • Projection period = age group size — we use 5-year age groups and project in 5-year steps, so each age group cohort moves up exactly one slot per step.
  • Closed to migration — all changes come from births and deaths only. (This is a modelling choice; migration can be added as an extra term.)
  • Exclusive — a woman can only be in one age group at a time (no ambiguity about age).

Notation

Let \(K(x, t)\) denote the number of women aged \(x\) at time \(t\). The subscript \(t-x\) then refers to the birth cohort — women who were born at time \(t - x\). So:

  • \(f(x, t-x)\) = fertility rate for girl babies of women aged \(x\) from birth cohort \(t-x\)
  • \(l(x, t-x)\) = survivorship from birth cohort \(t-x\) to age \(x\)

The key identity connecting these quantities: if \(B(t)\) = total births (girl babies) in year \(t\), then the number of women aged \(x\) in year \(t\) is just the number born \(x\) years ago, multiplied by the probability of surviving to age \(x\):

Cohort-component identity
\[ K(x,\,t) \;=\; B(t-x)\cdot l(x,\;t-x) \]

Read this as: "the women who are \(x\) years old today were born \(x\) years ago, and a fraction \(l(x, t-x)\) of them survived." As a specific example from the notes:

\(K(25, 2026)\) = women aged 25 in 2026 = \(B(2001) \cdot l(25, 2001)\). Women aged 25 in 2026 were born in 2001; the fraction that survived from birth to age 25 is \(l(25, 2001)\).

💡 Intuition — The Lexis Diagram Connection

The diagram Robert drew is just the Lexis diagram from Day 2, but with the arrows made explicit. Moving diagonally up-right along a cohort line represents women ageing through time. The 5-year boxes are the periods of the projection. Each arrow from age group \(x\) to age group \(x+5\) after 5 years carries a survivorship weight \({}_5p_x = l(x+5)/l(x)\).

The fertility arrows are different: they point from each age group downward to the 0–4 box, because women of each age can contribute births. These are the top row entries of the Leslie matrix.

6 The Renewal Equation: Births Today Depend on Past Births

How many girl babies are born in year \(t\)? Any woman who is age \(x\) in year \(t\) can contribute. The number aged \(x\) is \(K(x,t) = B(t-x)\cdot l(x,t-x)\), and their contribution to births is \(K(x,t)\cdot f(x, t-x)\). Integrating over all reproductive ages:

Adding up babies from all age groups
\[ \int_0^{100} K(x,t)\cdot f(x,t-x)\,dx \;=\; B(t) \]

Substituting the cohort-component identity \(K(x,t) = B(t-x)\cdot l(x,t-x)\):

The Renewal Equation (general form)
\[ B(t) \;=\; \int_0^\omega B(t-x)\underbrace{\vphantom{\Big|}l(x,\,t-x)}_{\text{survived}\atop\text{\(x\) years}}\underbrace{\vphantom{\Big|}f(x,\,t-x)}_{\text{gave kids}\atop\text{when age \(x\)}}\,dx \]

Here \(\omega\) is the oldest age of childbearing (around 50). This equation says: births today are a weighted sum of births in the past, with the weights given by survivorship and fertility. Robert emphasised: "This equation is important and ubiquitous." It appears in almost every area of mathematical biology and engineering under various names (renewal theory, Volterra integral equations).

📜 History — Renewal Theory

The renewal equation was first studied in a demographic context by Leonhard Euler in 1760 — long before Volterra or Lotka. The general theory of renewal processes was later developed by Alfred Lotka in the 1920s and 1930s, and again independently in the engineering literature in the context of reliability theory. The word "renewal" comes from the industrial analogy: if light bulbs burn out and are replaced (renewed), how many replacements happen per unit time? The demographic analogy: each birth is a "renewal" of the reproductive pool.

The Fixed-Rates Simplification

The general form allows \(l\) and \(f\) to vary by birth cohort — reflecting the real world where each generation faces different mortality and fertility. But right now, and for the rest of the day, we assume fixed rates: the same \(l(x)\) and \(f(x)\) apply to every birth cohort. This makes the notation cleaner and leads to the stable population theory:

Renewal equation — fixed rates (rates do not vary by cohort)
\[ B(t) \;=\; \int_0^\omega B(t-x)\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx \]

This simplification — replacing \(l(x, t-x)\) with just \(l(x)\) and \(f(x, t-x)\) with \(f(x)\) — is the key step that makes analytical progress possible. We are now in the world of the stable population model.

7 The Leslie Matrix: Organising the Arrows into a Machine

The renewal equation (and all those arrows in the box diagram) can be written compactly as a single matrix multiplication. Re-organise the arrows into a matrix where rows are age groups at time \(t+5\) and columns are age groups at time \(t\):

  • Top row (row 0): fertility coefficients — the number of girl babies produced by each age group. These are non-zero wherever a woman can have children (ages 15–49 roughly).
  • Sub-diagonal (A[i][i-1]): survivorship — the fraction of women in age group \(i-1\) who survive to become age group \(i\).
  • Zeros everywhere else: you can't jump age groups; a 20-year-old doesn't become a 35-year-old in one 5-year step.

📜 History — Leslie, Bernardelli, and Caswell

This structure is called the Leslie matrix after P.H. Leslie, who published the definitive paper in Biometrika in 1945. But the idea was independently discovered earlier by H. Bernardelli in 1941 (applied to beetle populations — demography and ecology have always shared tools). In population ecology, Hal Caswell's book Matrix Population Models became the standard reference, and Caswell's work got interleaved with Keyfitz's Applied Mathematical Demography in a productive collision of two bodies of literature.

The matrix approach transformed demography from a collection of numerical recipes into a coherent linear-algebraic theory, allowing all the power of eigenvalue analysis, sensitivity analysis, and Markov chain theory to be brought to bear.

8 Matrix Entries: Sub-Diagonal and Top Row

Sub-Diagonal: Survivorship

The sub-diagonal entry \(A_{i,i-1}\) is the fraction of women in age group \(x\) who survive to be in age group \(x+5\) five years later. With a life table, this is just the ratio of person-years in adjacent age groups:

Sub-diagonal entries (survivorship between age groups)
\[ A_{i,\,i-1} \;=\; \frac{{}_5L_{x+5}}{{}_5L_x} \]

Why \({}_5L_{x+5}/{}_5L_x\) rather than \(l(x+5)/l(x)\)? Because both the women entering and the women already in the 5-year group can die during the interval — the \(nL\) ratio correctly accounts for the exposure time within each group. From the Lexis diagram "zoomed-in view" on the board: the sub-diagonal represents the yellow arrow moving the cohort diagonally up-right.

📐 Note — Two Versions of the Survivorship Ratio

A simpler approximation for the sub-diagonal is \(l(x+5)/l(x)\) — the probability of surviving from exact age \(x\) to exact age \(x+5\). This differs from \({}_5L_{x+5}/{}_5L_x\) because \({}_5L_x\) is the average of the survivorship curve over the interval, not the survivorship at the exact age. In practice the two are very close, especially at older ages where mortality is smooth. The \(nL\) ratio is the more correct form when the projection period exactly equals the age-group width.

Top Row: Fertility Coefficients

The top row entry \(A_{0,j}\) is the number of girl babies born and surviving to age 0–4 per woman in age group \(j\) at the start of the period. A crude approximation just averages the fertility rates at the start and end of the interval:

\[ A_{0,j} \;\approx\; \frac{{}_5F_x + {}_5F_{x+5}}{2} \qquad \text{(simple approximation)} \]

The better entry from the notes, accounting for the mortality of mothers and the mortality of the babies themselves during the 0–4 period:

Top-row fertility entries — the better formula
\[ A_{0,j} \;=\; F_{\text{FAB}} \cdot \frac{l_0}{2}\!\left[F_x \;+\; F_{x+5}\cdot\frac{{}_5L_{x+5}}{{}_5L_x}\right] \cdot \frac{{}_5L_0}{l_0} \]

Breaking this apart:

  • \(F_{\text{FAB}}\) converts total births to female births (fraction female at birth ≈ 0.4886).
  • \(F_x + F_{x+5}\cdot{}_5L_{x+5}/{}_5L_x\) averages the fertility at the start and (projected) end of the interval, with the end weighted by survivorship of the mothers.
  • \({}_5L_0/l_0\) is the probability that a baby survives through the first 5-year period — accounting for infant and child mortality that whittles down the birth cohort before it reaches the next census.

In compact form (as in the R code from the Day 4 Activity sheet), with all age-groups at once:

Top row formula — compact R-style notation
\[ \mathbf{A}[1,\;] \;=\; F_{\text{FAB}}\cdot l_0 \cdot \frac{\mathbf{f}[1:n] \;+\; \mathbf{f}[2:(n+1)]\cdot\mathbf{l}[2:(n+1)]/\mathbf{l}[1:n]}{2} \]
where \(\mathbf{l}\) and \(\mathbf{f}\) are vectors of the Lx and Fx values (with \(l_0 = {}_5L_0\) in 5-year-group notation).

9 Demonstration: Fifty Projections with the USA 1934 Leslie Matrix

Robert's demonstration (a live R session, helped by course participants Wen and Felipe) used the USA 1934 life table and fertility schedule to construct a Leslie matrix \(\mathbf{A}\), then applied it to an arbitrary starting population — the Russian female age distribution — and projected 50 steps of 5 years each (250 years total).

The results: after some transient period, the growth rate stabilised at approximately \(r = 0.0157\;\text{yr}^{-1}\), and the age structure stabilised as well (approximately 14% in 0–4, 13% in 5–9, 6.5% in 45–49). This is called the stable state — a property that follows from deep linear algebra.

🌍 Real World — Mixing Rates from Different Countries

The demonstration deliberately mixed USA 1934 mortality and fertility with a Russian initial age structure. This is not a realistic projection of Russia — it's a mathematical demonstration that the stable state depends only on the rates (the Leslie matrix), not on the starting population. Whatever starting distribution you choose, after enough steps you converge to the same stable age structure and the same \(r\). The initial conditions are "forgotten." This is the demographic version of the ergodic theorem: the long-run behaviour is determined by the rates alone.

10 The Perron–Frobenius Theorem and Eigenvalues

Why does the Leslie matrix always converge to a stable state? The answer is the Perron–Frobenius theorem, a foundational result in linear algebra that applies to non-negative matrices with certain irreducibility properties.

📖 Definition — Perron–Frobenius Theorem (demographic statement)

A Leslie matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) that is primitive (roughly: the population is not split into isolated sub-populations that never interbreed) has a unique dominant real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1 > 0\) that is strictly larger than all other eigenvalues in absolute value. The corresponding eigenvector has all positive entries and gives the stable age distribution. Long-run population growth is determined solely by \(\lambda_1\), with intrinsic growth rate \(r = \ln(\lambda_1)/n\) where \(n\) is the projection interval.

A 10×10 Leslie matrix has 10 eigenvalues (counting multiplicity). The largest real one gives \(r\); all the others are smaller and their contributions decay away over time. The complex eigenvalues cause the oscillations you see in the growth rate in Widget 3 before it stabilises — they correspond to periodic fluctuations in age structure driven by echo effects of past fertility waves.

The Complex Eigenvalues and Euler's Formula

A complex eigenvalue \(\lambda = a + ib\) contributes a term to the population trajectory that oscillates over time. Why? Because of Euler's formula. Recall:

Euler's formula (the bridge between exponentials and trigonometry)
\[ e^{i\theta} \;=\; \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \]

So the growth factor \(e^{\lambda t} = e^{(a+ib)t} = e^{at}\cdot e^{ibt}\) has a real growing/decaying part \(e^{at}\) and a sinusoidal oscillating part \(e^{ibt} = \cos(bt) + i\sin(bt)\). The complex eigenvalues of the Leslie matrix produce exactly these oscillations in the population over time — "echo effects" from past baby booms rippling through the age structure.

📜 History — Euler's Identity

The special case \(\theta = \pi\) gives \(e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1 + 0 = -1\), usually written as \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\). This is often called Euler's identity and voted the most beautiful equation in mathematics — it connects the five fundamental constants \(e, i, \pi, 1, 0\) in a single relation. Richard Feynman called it "the most remarkable formula in mathematics." In our context, this identity says that the complex part of a Leslie eigenvalue at period \(\pi\) of its oscillation contributes a factor of exactly \(-1\) — a perfect 180° phase flip — to the age structure.

The lecture summarised this elegantly: "The eigenvalues, whether real or complex, are the exponents in the exponential growth model \(e^{rt}\). The complex parts look like \(e^{i\pi t}\)", meaning they oscillate. The eigendecomposition of the Leslie matrix is therefore a compact summary of everything encoded in the \({}_nL_x\)'s and \({}_nF_x\)'s.

11 The Stable State and Its Properties

In the stable state, the entire population grows at the same constant rate \(r\). Not just the total — every age group grows at rate \(r\), the number of births grows at rate \(r\), and the age distribution (the proportions) is constant over time. As Robert put it: imagine a pie chart that grows but keeps the same slice proportions.

The Mathematical Conditions

If the population is in stable state and growing at rate \(r\), then advancing by \(n\) years just multiplies the population by \(e^{rn}\):

Stable state: each age group grows at the same rate r
\[\begin{aligned} K(x,\,t+n) &= K(x,\,t)\cdot e^{rn} \\[4pt] B(t+n) &= B(t)\cdot e^{rn} \\[4pt] B(t-x) &= B(t)\cdot e^{-rx} \end{aligned}\]

The last line is the key: in stable state, births \(x\) years ago are related to births today by a simple factor \(e^{-rx}\). This is exactly what the stable state assumption says — births have been growing at rate \(r\) all along.

💡 Intuition — The Growing Pie

Imagine two pie charts side by side. The right one is bigger (total population is larger) but the slices are the same proportions (same age distribution). Moving from today's pie to tomorrow's (5 years later), every slice — every age group — has grown by the same factor \(e^{5r}\). Even the births-slice (0–4) has grown by the same factor. This is the meaning of the stable state: all age groups "forget" their initial conditions and lock into the same growth rate, determined by the Leslie matrix.

12 The Euler–Lotka Equation: The Most Important Equation

Now the payoff. If the population is in stable state with births growing at rate \(r\), we can substitute \(B(t-x) = B(t)e^{-rx}\) directly into the renewal equation (with fixed rates):

Deriving the Euler–Lotka equation — step by step
\[\begin{aligned} B(t) &= \int_0^\omega B(t-x)\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx \\[4pt] &= \int_0^\omega B(t)\,e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx && \text{(stable state: } B(t-x) = B(t)e^{-rx}\text{)} \\[4pt] &= B(t)\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx && \text{(factor out } B(t)\text{)} \\[4pt] 1 &= \int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx && \text{(divide both sides by }B(t)\text{)} \end{aligned}\]
⭐ The Euler–Lotka Equation (1760) — "The most important equation in all of formal demography"
\[1 \;=\; \int_0^\omega e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\,dx\]
Where \(r\) is the intrinsic growth rate, \(l(x)\) is the survival function, \(f(x)\) is the age-specific fertility rate, and \(\omega\) is the age beyond which no births occur.

The remarkable thing is what this equation does: it implicitly defines the intrinsic rate of growth \(r\) entirely in terms of the life table \(l(x)\) and the fertility schedule \(f(x)\). You don't need to observe any population dynamics directly — you just need age-specific rates, and \(r\) is the unique real number that makes this integral equal to 1.

📜 History — Euler and Lotka

Leonhard Euler first derived this equation in 1760, in a paper analysing an annuity scheme — a problem about compounding interest rates that turned out to be mathematically equivalent to stable population theory. He was 53 years old and had already been largely blind for nearly two decades.

Alfred J. Lotka independently rediscovered it around 1907 (published fully in the 1920s) in the context of biological populations, and proved the existence and uniqueness of a real solution \(r\). Lotka also coined the term "intrinsic rate of natural increase." The equation is now often called the Lotka characteristic equation, or the Euler–Lotka equation.

Lotka's 1925 book Elements of Physical Biology was perhaps the first unified mathematical theory of ecology and demography. He spent his career at the Metropolitan Life Insurance Company — a reminder that actuarial science and demography have always been intertwined.

💡 Intuition — What Does the Integral Say?

Think of \(e^{-rx}\,l(x)\,f(x)\) as the discounted net maternity function. The term \(l(x)f(x)\) is the net maternity function from Day 3 — the expected (female) births per woman at age \(x\). But we discount it by \(e^{-rx}\): births that happen far in the future (large \(x\)) are worth less in present-value terms because the population has grown by a factor \(e^{rx}\) in the meantime. The Euler–Lotka equation says: the present value of all future (female) births per woman, discounted at rate \(r\), must equal exactly 1. This is a biological "break-even" condition — each woman exactly replaces herself in present-value terms.

⚠ Common Mistake — Confusing NRR with the Euler–Lotka Condition

The NRR is \(\int l(x)\,f(x)\,dx\cdot F_\text{FAB}\) — the integral of the net maternity function with no discounting (equivalently, the Euler–Lotka integral evaluated at \(r=0\)). The Euler–Lotka equation is the same integral but discounted by \(e^{-rx}\) and set equal to 1. So NRR > 1 ⟺ \(r > 0\) (positive growth) and NRR = 1 ⟺ \(r = 0\) (stationary), but NRR is not the same as the Euler–Lotka integral in general. NRR tells you the direction of growth; \(r\) tells you the speed.

13 The Stable Age Distribution and the Stationary Population

Now we can work out what the stable age structure looks like. In stable state, \(K(x,t) = B(t-x)\cdot l(x)\), and in stable state \(B(t-x) = B(t)\cdot e^{-rx}\), so:

\[ K(x,t) = B(t)\,e^{-rx}\,l(x) \] The total population is \(K(t) = \int_0^\omega K(x,t)\,dx = B(t)\int_0^\omega e^{-rx}l(x)\,dx\). So the proportion aged \(x\) is:
Stable age distribution
\[ c(x) \;=\; \frac{K(x,t)}{K(t)} \;=\; b\,\cdot l(x)\,\cdot e^{-rx} \]
where \(b\) is the crude birth rate, defined by the normalisation condition: \[ \int_0^\omega c(x)\,dx = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; b = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{-rx}\,dx} \]
📖 Definition — Stable Age Distribution

The stable age distribution is \(c(x) = b\,l(x)\,e^{-rx}\), where \(b\) is the crude birth rate normalised so that \(\int c(x)\,dx = 1\). Key features:
— In a growing population (\(r > 0\)): the \(e^{-rx}\) discounting makes the distribution younger — more weight at low \(x\).
— In a declining population (\(r < 0\)): the \(e^{rx}\) amplification makes the distribution older.
— In a stationary population (\(r = 0\)): the distribution is simply \(c(x) = b\cdot l(x)\), proportional to survivorship.

The Special Case: Stationary Population and \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\)

Set \(r = 0\). The stable age distribution becomes \(c(x) = b\cdot l(x)\), and the normalisation gives:

\[ \frac{1}{b} = \int_0^\omega l(x)\,e^{0}\,dx = \int_0^\omega l(x)\,dx = e_0 \] so \(b\cdot e_0 = 1\). But this is exactly the identity from Day 1 — the relationship between the crude birth rate and life expectancy in a stationary population! We have now derived it from first principles as a consequence of the stable population model.

📐 Full Circle: From Day 1 to Day 4

On Day 1, the identity \(b \cdot e_0 = 1\) (or equivalently \(\bar{b} \cdot T = N\) in the Lexis diagram) was presented as an empirical observation and intuitive fact. Now, at the end of Day 4, it emerges as a theorem: a stationary population is a stable population with \(r = 0\), and in that case the Euler–Lotka normalisation gives exactly \(b\cdot e_0 = 1\). The Day 1 result was never just a coincidence — it was stable population theory in disguise.

🌍 Real World — Momentum and the Stable State

Most real populations are not in stable state — they have "too many" or "too few" young people relative to what the current rates would eventually produce. A country that has recently reduced its fertility (like China after the one-child policy) has an age structure built up from the high-fertility past, meaning it still has many young women who will have children even at the new lower rates. The population keeps growing for decades after fertility drops to replacement level. This is population momentum — one of the topics for Day 5 — and it is entirely explained by the gap between the current age structure and the eventual stable age structure.
